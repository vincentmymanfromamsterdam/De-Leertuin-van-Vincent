# Olbers' paradox

## Core
Why is the night sky dark? If the universe is infinitely large, infinitely old, and more or less uniformly filled with stars, it shouldn't be. Something in those premises is wrong — and that something tells us how the universe really hangs together.

## Explanation
Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers, a German doctor and astronomer, formulated the problem in 1823. He wasn't the first — Kepler, Halley, and Cheseaux had made similar observations — but Olbers' version stuck, hence the eponym.

The reasoning is beautifully simple. Imagine standing in an infinitely dense forest. In whatever direction you look, your eye eventually meets a tree trunk. Replace the trees with stars and you have Olbers' argument: in an infinite universe full of stars, *every* line of sight from Earth eventually terminates on the surface of a star. The night sky should therefore be as bright as the surface of the Sun. It isn't. The question is: why not?

The escape comes through two doors, both needed.

The first is that the universe is not infinitely old. It is about 13.8 billion years old. Light travels at finite speed. From stars further away than 13.8 billion light-years, the light has simply not reached us. We are not only looking through space, we are looking through time — and time runs out. The [[Big Bang]] sets a limit on what we can ever see, and that limit spares our nights.

The second is expansion. The universe is expanding, so light from distant objects is redshifted and, in the extreme, shifted entirely out of the visible spectrum. What remains of the glow of the early universe we now find as the cosmic microwave background — a form of the "bright sky" Olbers predicted, displaced into microwaves invisible to the naked eye. With a radio telescope on the right frequency, the entire sky *does* glow. Olbers was right; only the senses we look with have the wrong sensitivity.

A curious detail: Edgar Allan Poe, not a scientist but a poet, intuited the correct answer in his essay *Eureka* (1848) — without the mathematics, on poetic reasoning alone. The history of science is occasionally moving.

## Why this matters
Olbers' paradox is one of the finest examples of armchair philosophy predicting physics. With only logic and the assumption that light has a finite speed, you can argue that a static infinite universe is impossible. That is a different route to the same conclusion as Hubble's expansion and Einstein's field equations. Three completely independent paths all lead to the Big Bang model — which, statistically, is a strong sign that the model is right.

## Open questions
- If the universe keeps expanding forever, will the night actually grow darker in the very distant future as galaxies disappear beyond the horizon?
- Is there an analogous "gravitational Olbers' paradox" — why aren't we crushed by the gravity of an infinite universe?
