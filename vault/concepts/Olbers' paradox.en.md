# Olbers' paradox

## The question
Why is the night sky dark?

That sounds trivial — until you think it through. If the universe is infinitely large, infinitely old, and more or less uniformly filled with stars, then *every* line of sight from Earth eventually terminates on the surface of a star. The night sky should therefore be as bright as the surface of the Sun. It isn't. Something in the premises is wrong.

## Who and when
Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers, a German doctor and astronomer, formulated the problem in 1823. He wasn't the first — Kepler, Halley, and Cheseaux had made similar observations earlier. It was Olbers' version that stuck, hence the eponym. Edgar Allan Poe, in his essay *Eureka* (1848), intuited the correct answer without the mathematics.

## The resolution
The escape comes through two doors, both needed.

**Finite age.** The universe is not infinitely old. It is about 13.8 billion years old. Light travels at finite speed. From stars further away than 13.8 billion light-years, the light has simply not reached us. We are not only looking through space, we are looking through time — and time runs out.

**Expansion.** The universe is expanding, so light from distant objects is redshifted and, in the extreme, shifted entirely out of the visible spectrum. What remains of the glow of the early universe we now find as the cosmic microwave background — a form of the "bright sky" Olbers predicted, displaced into microwaves invisible to the naked eye.

## Why this matters
Olbers' paradox is one of the finest examples of armchair-philosophy predicting physics. With only logic and the assumption that light has a finite speed, you can argue that a static infinite universe is impossible. That is a different route to the same conclusion as Hubble-Lemaître's expansion and Einstein's field equations. Three completely independent paths all lead to the Big Bang model.
