# Earthworms as soil builders

## Core
Darwin devoted his last great work not to evolution but to earthworms. He calculated that in England they pass the entire topsoil through their bodies every year. They are the quietest and most underestimated architects of any garden ecosystem.

## Explanation
Charles Darwin published *The Formation of Vegetable Mould through the Action of Worms* in 1881 — his bestselling book during his lifetime. His conclusion: earthworms are the most important factor in forming and maintaining fertile soil. That was a revolutionary idea in his day, and it remains true today.

A healthy Dutch garden soil contains 100–400 earthworms per square metre. They do three things no other organism does as effectively.

First: mixing. Worms pull organic matter (leaves, dead roots) downward and bring mineral soil upward. This prevents stratification and produces a homogeneous, fertile topsoil. It is exactly what digging also does, but worms do it continuously, without destroying the structure.

Second: aerating. Worm tunnels create a network of channels through which water infiltrates and roots can grow deeper. A soil with strong worm activity absorbs rain much faster than a compacted soil — relevant given the increasing intensity of rainfall under climate change.

Third: enriching. Worm castings contain five times more nitrogen, seven times more phosphate, and eleven times more potassium than the surrounding soil. They are also coated with mucus that feeds microorganisms — perfect starting points for [[Mycorrhiza]] networks.

There are three ecological groups of earthworms, each playing a different role. Litter dwellers (red, small, living in the top layer — the compost worm belongs here) process surface material. Soil dwellers (pink, medium-sized) mix the upper 20 cm. Deep burrowers (large, dark-headed — the common earthworm) make vertical tunnels more than a metre deep and pull leaves down.

What kills worms: digging (destroys tunnels and worms physically), artificial fertilisers (acidifies the soil), pesticides (direct toxicity), and bare ground without organic matter (no food). What promotes worms: mulching, composting, no-dig methods, and moist soil rich in organic matter.

## Why this matters
If Darwin is right — and 140 years of research confirms he is — then the earthworm is the foundation of your garden. No worm, no healthy soil. No healthy soil, no healthy plant. It is the most direct chain in garden ecology, and the easiest to influence: stop digging, mulch, and the worms do the rest.
