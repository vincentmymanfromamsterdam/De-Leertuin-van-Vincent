# Water as the lifeline of the garden

## Core
A water feature — even a shallow dish or half a barrel — is the single element that raises a garden's biodiversity most quickly and most dramatically. Water draws in life that simply would not be there without it.

## Explanation
Within weeks of installing a pond or even a large container of water, residents you didn't invite begin to appear: pond skaters (beetles that skate over the surface), damselflies, dragonflies, backswimmers, and in the evening amphibians attracted by the sound of water. That is no coincidence — water is the missing element in most gardens, and many species depend on it entirely for at least one phase of their life cycle.

Dragonflies and damselflies lay their eggs in or near water. Their larvae spend one to three years underwater as formidable predators (they eat mosquito larvae — a welcome side-effect). The common toad, common frog, and smooth newt need a pond to mate and lay eggs, but spend most of the year on land, in damp corners with leaf litter and log piles.

Birds need water for drinking and bathing. A shallow edge (max 5 cm deep) with a rough surface is ideal — birds are vulnerable while bathing and want to be able to escape quickly. A sloping bank or a stone protruding just above the water makes the difference.

Even the plants around a pond are ecologically valuable: water forget-me-not, purple loosestrife, watercress, and bulrush offer nectar to pollinators and shelter to insects.

The parallel with [[Water as the primordial substance|Thales]] is coincidental but fitting: water as primordial principle, as that from which life emerges. In the context of a garden that is almost literally true — add water and life appears.

Practical tips: avoid fish in a small pond (they eat all the larvae and eggs), don't use tap water (chlorine) but rainwater, ensure planted edges and a deep section (at least 60 cm) so amphibians can overwinter without freezing.

## Why this matters
A garden without water is like a city without parks: it works, but a whole dimension is missing. Water adds not just a few species but a whole ecological guild — amphibians, dragonflies, aquatic macrofauna — that without water is simply absent.

## Open questions
- How small can a water feature be and still be ecologically meaningful? Is a bucket enough for mosquito-larvae-eating waterlife?
- How do you handle the inevitable algal bloom in the first year?
