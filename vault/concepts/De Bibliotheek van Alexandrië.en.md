# The Library of Alexandria

## Core
The Library of Alexandria was the greatest centre of knowledge in the ancient world — a place where the entire known knowledge of humanity was gathered, copied, and studied. Its gradual destruction stands as a symbol for the fragility of civilisation and knowledge.

## Explanation
The Library was founded around 300 BCE by Ptolemy I, one of Alexander the Great's successors, in the Egyptian port city of Alexandria. At its peak it held an estimated 400,000–700,000 scrolls — the bulk of all written knowledge in the Greek, Egyptian, Persian, and Babylonian worlds.

It was more than a storehouse. The Library was a research institute: scholars were paid to work, debate, and produce new work there. Eratosthenes calculated the circumference of the Earth there (with only 2% error). Aristarchus proposed the heliocentric model — 1,800 years before Copernicus. Euclid wrote his *Elements*. Hipparchus catalogued the stars. It was the closest approximation to a university the ancient world produced.

Sagan devotes an entire chapter of *Cosmos* to the Library and describes its destruction as one of the greatest intellectual catastrophes in human history. The details of the destruction are historically unclear — it was probably not a single fire but a gradual decline over centuries, accelerated by political unrest, religious conflict, and neglect. What is clear: most of the work of the pre-Socratics, of [[Democritus and atomism|Democritus]], of hundreds of other thinkers, has been lost. We know their ideas only through fragments and summaries by later authors.

Russell's treatment of Greek philosophy illustrates this loss indirectly: nearly everything he writes about the pre-Socratics comes from secondary sources — Aristotle quoting Thales, Simplicius quoting Anaximander. The original works are gone.

Sagan draws a line to the present: knowledge that is not actively maintained, copied, and passed on, disappears. That held for papyrus scrolls in antiquity and holds for digital data today. The Library of Alexandria is not only history but a warning.

## Why this matters
It reminds us that progress is not inevitable. A civilisation can lose knowledge, and recovering it can take centuries — if it happens at all. The Dark Ages following the fall of Rome are partly the consequence of the loss that began in Alexandria.

## Open questions
- How differently would we understand the pre-Socratics if their original works had survived?
- Is the internet our modern Library of Alexandria — and is it just as fragile?
