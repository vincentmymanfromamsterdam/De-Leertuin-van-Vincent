# Why didn't the early universe become a black hole?

## Core
In the first fractions of a second after the Big Bang, all the energy of the visible universe was packed into a minuscule volume. At first glance that sounds like the perfect recipe for a black hole. Yet the universe expanded rather than collapsed. The reason is that a black hole does not arise from a lot of energy in a small place, but from a density difference — and in the early universe every cubic centimetre was as dense as every other.

## Explanation
The intuition "lots of mass in a small place = black hole" holds locally but not globally. A black hole does not form because energy is concentrated somewhere, but because **outside that region there is much less energy**. The singularity arises from the difference — a dense core in a less dense surrounding, so that matter is pulled harder from one side than the other. Without that density difference there is no preferred direction for "inward".

In the early universe density was homogeneous everywhere. Every location was pulled equally from all sides — net force zero. No collapsing core, no forming horizon. Newton already saw this principle, wrestling with the question of why an infinite universe full of stars does not collapse: in a uniform medium there is no "inward" anywhere.

The second part of the answer lies in the **direction of time**. In the Schwarzschild solution for a black hole, time inside the horizon is tilted so that the singularity lies in the future. Anything that falls in can do nothing but head toward the singularity — just as you can do nothing but head toward tomorrow. But in the standard cosmological metric (FLRW), the singularity of the Big Bang lies in the **past**. Expansion is built into the very structure of spacetime. The universe was born with an outward-pointing motion — mathematically the exact mirror image of a black hole. A black hole is a spacetime where everything converges; the Big Bang is a spacetime where everything diverges.

The third part comes from **inflation**. Between about 10⁻³⁶ and 10⁻³² seconds after the Big Bang, the universe expanded by at least a factor of 10²⁶ in a tiny span of time. The inflaton field drove that exponential expansion with negative pressure — similar to dark energy but enormously stronger. During inflation a repulsive force dominated over all attraction. Even if there were local density fluctuations that wanted to collapse, inflation pulled them apart so quickly that they could never form a black hole. At the end of inflation the inflaton field decayed into the familiar particles and ordinary gravitational physics returned — but by that time the universe was so vast that local collapse could only occur much later, on small scale, in stars.

At the deepest level the story is summarised in the Friedmann equation, which for a homogeneous universe relates the expansion rate H to the energy density ρ:

H² = (8πG/3) × ρ − kc²/a²

In the early universe ρ was enormous — but that drove H upward, not downward. High energy density in this dynamic context means **fast expansion**, not collapse. This is the subtlety of general relativity: the same energy that produces gravity also drives expansion once the system is in an expanding solution. A static dense ball would collapse; an expanding dense ball can keep expanding, provided expansion is faster than attraction can stop it.

There is an honest caveat here. What really happened in the very first Planck units — before 10⁻⁴³ seconds — we do not know. General relativity predicts infinite density and curvature there, which is physically nonsensical. Possibly there was never a true singularity but only a state of extreme yet finite density; possibly the Big Bang was the end of a previous collapsing universe, a "Big Bounce"; possibly the initial condition was something altogether different that we cannot yet describe. What we *do* know: from the moment our theories become workable, the universe was already in an expanding state, not a collapsing one. The outward impulse was already there.

## Why this matters
This question shows how radically differently general relativity views gravity than Newton did. For Newton, gravity was a force that always pulled inward, and the Big Bang would inevitably have had to collapse. For Einstein, gravity is a curvature of spacetime that can just as well point outward as inward, depending on the global geometry. The fact that our universe exists, that we exist at all, is therefore not a chance survival of a collapsing black hole — it is a direct consequence of what spacetime can dynamically do when it expands as a whole. The Big Bang and a black hole are mathematically each other's mirror image: same equations, different boundary conditions, different direction of time.

## Open questions
- What really happened during the Planck time? Was there ever a true singularity, or is that an artefact of an incomplete theory?
- Was the Big Bang truly unique, or is it the end of an earlier collapsing phase (Big Bounce)?
- Could there be regions in our universe where the balance tipped — primordial black holes that *did* form in the earliest phases? Some theories suggest such objects could account for dark matter.
