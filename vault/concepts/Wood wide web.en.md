# The wood wide web

## Kern
Beneath the soil of every forest lies a network of fungal threads connecting trees to one another. Through this network, trees exchange sugars, nutrients and even warning signals — an underground internet of the forest.

## Uitleg
The concept of mycorrhiza describes the symbiosis between one plant and one fungal network. But mycorrhizal fungi don't limit themselves to a single tree. Their mycelial threads connect to the roots of multiple trees simultaneously, sometimes dozens, often of different species. The result is a network that spans the entire forest floor.

Sheldrake describes in *Entangled Life* how carbon (sugars) can flow through these networks from a tree catching plenty of sunlight to one in the shade. Mother trees — large, old trees with extensive root systems — appear to feed their seedlings via the fungal network, giving young trees a higher survival rate.

Even more remarkable: when a tree is attacked by insects, it can release chemical signals that travel through the mycelium to neighbouring trees. Those trees then begin producing defensive compounds preventively, before the insects even reach them. The forest responds as a collective, not as a collection of individuals.

The term "wood wide web" is popular but not uncontroversial in science. Sheldrake himself is cautious: the metaphor of an "internet" suggests conscious communication, while in reality these are chemical processes. The fungus is not an altruistic postman but an organism serving its own interest — it delivers nutrients to trees that give it sugars in return. The "communication" is a by-product of that transaction.

Yet it fundamentally changes how we look at a forest: not as an arena where trees compete for light, but as a community connected underground by a fungal network that may be older than the trees themselves.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
It shifts the perspective from individual to network. A tree is not an independent organism but a node in a web of relationships. This echoes Heraclitus: everything is connected, and the connection is more real than the separate parts.

## Open vragen
- To what extent does this network also function in a garden with fruit trees and shrubs, or is it primarily a forest phenomenon?
- If the fungus manages the network, who has the "power" — the tree or the fungus?
