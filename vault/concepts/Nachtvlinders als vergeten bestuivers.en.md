# Moths as forgotten pollinators

## Kern
Moths pollinate more plant species than butterflies and bees combined, yet are almost never mentioned in conversations about pollinators. They work the night shift, invisible and underestimated.

## Uitleg
The Netherlands has approximately 2,500 moth species compared to just 55 butterfly species. That ratio — 45:1 — already indicates how much diversity we overlook. Recent studies have shown that moths visit a broader spectrum of plant species than daytime pollinators and transport pollen over greater distances.

Moths pollinate plants specifically attuned to them: white or pale flowers that open in the evening and produce strong scents (honeysuckle, night-flowering catchfly, evening primrose). That scent is their equivalent of the bright colours that attract butterflies — moths navigate by smell, not sight.

The problem: light pollution. Street lights, garden spotlights and illuminated windows draw moths away from flowers toward artificial light, where they circle exhausted until they die or are eaten. Research shows that areas with more artificial light have measurably less pollination by moths.

What you can do: plant night-blooming species (wild honeysuckle, night-flowering catchfly), avoid garden lighting or use warm-white LED with a timer, and leave an untidy corner with grass tussocks — many moth caterpillars hide during the day at the base of grasses.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
We optimise gardens for daytime pollinators and forget that the night has a complete second pollination shift. Reducing light pollution in your garden is one of the simplest ways to promote biodiversity — it literally costs nothing, you just need to turn off a lamp.
