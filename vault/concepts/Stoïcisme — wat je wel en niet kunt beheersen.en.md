# Stoicism — what you can and cannot control

## Core
The Stoics held that happiness does not depend on what happens to you but on how you respond to it. The only thing truly within your power is your own judgement — everything else (health, possessions, reputation, even death) lies outside your control and therefore deserves no emotional investment.

## Explanation
Stoicism arose around 300 BCE in Athens, founded by Zeno of Citium (not to be confused with [[Zeno's paradoxes|Zeno of Elea]]). It grew into one of the most influential philosophical schools of antiquity and counted among its followers both a slave (Epictetus) and an emperor (Marcus Aurelius).

The core idea is the dichotomy of control. Divide everything into two categories: things within your power (your judgements, your intentions, your reactions) and things that are not (the weather, the opinions of others, illness, death). Direct all your energy toward the first category and accept the second with equanimity — *apatheia*, not insensitivity but freedom from destructive emotions.

The Stoics saw the universe as one rational whole, governed by the *logos* — a term they shared with [[Heraclitus and the logos|Heraclitus]]. Everything that happens, happens according to a cosmic reason. Resisting the course of things is like a dog tied to a moving cart: you can run along or you can be dragged. The result is the same; only your experience differs.

Russell acknowledges the ethical force of Stoicism but criticises the passivity that can follow from it. If everything that happens is "the will of fate", why fight injustice? The Stoics had an answer to this — you have a duty to act justly, but you must let go of the result — but Russell finds this intellectually unsatisfying.

What makes Stoicism particularly relevant is that it is the first philosophy explicitly presented as an art of living: not a theory about the world but a daily practice. Epictetus's *Encheiridion* is essentially a manual for mental resilience — and the direct precursor of modern cognitive behavioural therapy.

## Why this matters
Of all ancient philosophies, Stoicism is the most directly applicable in daily life. The dichotomy of control is a thinking tool you can use in the face of stress, setbacks, and situations you cannot change — precisely the situations that fill the life of a chiropractor with three small children.

## Open questions
- Is Stoicism too passive? Where is the line between acceptance and resignation?
- How does the Stoic *logos* relate to modern natural laws? Both suggest a rationally ordered universe.
