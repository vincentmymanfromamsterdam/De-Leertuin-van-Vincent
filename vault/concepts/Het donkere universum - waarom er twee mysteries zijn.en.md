# The dark universe: why there are two mysteries

## Core
95% of the universe is "dark" — invisible to light and all electromagnetic radiation. But that 95% consists of two fundamentally different things acting in opposite directions on spacetime. Dark matter pulls inward and holds galaxies together. Dark energy pushes outward and accelerates the expansion of the universe. One universe, two mysteries, two opposing accounts still open in physics.

## Explanation
According to the most recent measurements from the Planck satellite, the universe consists of:

| Component | Share | Effect on gravity |
|---|---|---|
| Ordinary matter (atoms) | 5% | Attractive |
| Dark matter | 27% | Attractive |
| Dark energy | 68% | Repulsive (negative pressure) |

Stars, planets, gas, dust, you, me, everything we have ever observed with light or instruments, therefore makes up just one twentieth of the total energy content. The other nineteen twentieths reveal themselves only through gravity.

**Dark matter came into view first.** Vera Rubin measured in the 1970s how fast stars in spiral galaxies orbit their centre. By Newton's laws the outer stars should orbit more slowly than inner ones, just as Pluto orbits the Sun more slowly than Mercury. But observations showed a flat rotation curve: outer stars orbit just as fast as inner ones, sometimes even faster. With only the visible mass there is not enough gravity to keep those outer stars in orbit — they should fly out of the galaxy. The solution: an invisible halo of extra mass surrounding every galaxy. That mass does not interact with light (hence "dark"), but does interact with gravity.

**Dark energy came later, and from a completely different direction.** In 1998 two independent teams of astronomers discovered that distant supernovae were dimmer than predicted. The only explanation: the universe is not expanding more slowly, as gravity would suggest, but ever faster. Something is pushing spacetime apart. At cosmological scales, that outward pressure beats the attraction of all the matter combined.

**The crucial difference lies in pressure.** Einstein's gravity equation couples not only to energy density but also to pressure. Positive pressure (ordinary gas, photons) contributes to attractive gravity. Negative pressure does the opposite: it pushes space apart. Dark energy has negative pressure equal to its energy density — precisely the amount needed to accelerate expansion. Dark matter has no such peculiarity and behaves gravitationally like ordinary mass.

**Because of this, the two are not interchangeable.** Dark energy cannot explain galaxy rotation curves — its density is so low (about 10⁻²⁷ kg/m³) that its contribution within a galaxy is negligible, and the direction is wrong: it would push stars outward, not hold them in orbit. Conversely, dark matter cannot explain the accelerating expansion — it pulls, it does not push. The universe needs both, on different scales, with opposing effects.

**A spectacular piece of evidence that dark matter is not a calculation error.** In 2006 astronomers observed the Bullet Cluster: two colliding clusters of galaxies. The visible gas (mapped via X-ray emission) collided and stayed in the middle. The total mass (measured via gravitational lensing) was elsewhere — on both sides of the collision, as if it had flown straight through the gas. That is exactly what you'd expect if mass consists of particles that don't interact with electromagnetism but do feel gravity. The visible matter clashed; the dark matter passed through unhindered.

**What might dark matter be?** The leading candidates are axions (extremely light hypothetical particles) and WIMPs (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles). Decades of experiments with ever more sensitive detectors in deep underground laboratories have yet to find a convincing signal. Dark energy remains even more mysterious: the simplest explanation is a **cosmological constant** — a property of empty space itself — but calculations from quantum mechanics give a value that is 10¹²⁰ times too large. That is the largest discrepancy between prediction and measurement in all of science.

## Why this matters
The fact that 95% of the universe is unknown is not a detail still to be filled in — it is the biggest open question in modern physics. Our entire inventory of what exists, built up over four centuries of science since Newton, covers just one twentieth of what is actually there. And what remains points to physics we do not yet understand: new particles we cannot catch, or a fundamental property of empty space our theories cannot predict. It is conceivable that we live in a time comparable to that just before the quantum revolution of 1900 — when everyone thought physics was largely complete, and then it turned out there were two small puzzles that eventually changed everything.

## Open questions
- Does dark matter consist of one type of particle or of an entire "dark sector" with its own forces and families of particles?
- Is dark energy truly constant, or does its density change over time? DESI measurements from 2024 may suggest the latter — which would shake the standard model of cosmology.
- Is the picture of two separate mysteries correct, or are dark matter and dark energy two manifestations of one underlying phenomenon we have not yet glimpsed?
