# From potentiality to actuality — superposition, symmetry breaking and the birth of reality

## Kern
Both quantum superposition and symmetry breaking describe the same fundamental transition: from all possibilities at once to one realised reality. Aristotle had a word for this 2,400 years ago — the transition from *potentiality* to *actuality* — making this one of the deepest connections between philosophy and modern physics.

## Uitleg
Aristotle introduced the pair *dynamis* (potentiality, possibility) and *energeia* (actuality, reality) as a solution to an ancient problem. Parmenides had argued that change is logically impossible: something cannot arise from Being (because then it already existed) or from Non-Being (because nothing comes from nothing). Aristotle's answer: there is an intermediate state. An acorn is not an oak, but it is also not "nothing" — it is an oak *in potentiality*. Change is the transition from potentiality to actuality.

Russell treats this as one of Aristotle's most important contributions: it makes change thinkable without falling into Parmenides' trap. But Russell also criticises the vagueness — what is "potentiality" exactly? It sounds like a property of things, but isn't it rather a property of our description?

Remarkably, modern physics has two phenomena that almost literally realise the potentiality-actuality structure.

**Quantum superposition** is the first. An electron in the double-slit experiment exists before measurement in a superposition — it is not "at slit A" or "at slit B" but in a state encompassing all possibilities simultaneously. This is not a lack of knowledge (we don't know where it is), but the nature of reality at quantum scale: the particle *is* all possibilities at once. It is pure potentiality, in Aristotelian terms.

Upon measurement, the wave function collapses to one outcome. The particle appears at slit A or slit B. Potentiality becomes actuality. But the process is mysterious — no one knows precisely how or why the "choice" is made. This is the measurement problem, one of the deepest open questions in physics.

**Symmetry breaking** is the second phenomenon, operating at a different scale. The Higgs field in the early universe had no preference — all directions in the abstract field space were equivalent. The system was symmetric: all possibilities existed in potentiality. As the universe cooled, the field "chose" one direction. The symmetry broke, and the world we know — with mass, with different forces, with structure — appeared. Potentiality became actuality.

The analogy is tempting, but there are crucial differences. In quantum superposition, all possibilities *genuinely exist* simultaneously — this is not a metaphor. In symmetry breaking, all possibilities *existed* as equivalent options, but the choice is definitive and thermodynamic: the system falls to its lowest energy state, which happens to be asymmetric. No observer is needed — only cooling.

Another way to see the difference: in quantum collapse (in the standard interpretation), the unchosen possibilities genuinely disappear. In symmetry breaking, they are still there — hidden in the mathematics of the laws. The pencil could have fallen the other way. The symmetry has not vanished but is hidden.

Yet both processes share a deeper structure: the transition from a state with maximum possibilities (high symmetry, superposition) to a state with fewer possibilities but more structure (broken symmetry, measured outcome). And that is precisely what Aristotle meant: reality is what remains when possibilities narrow to one.

Carroll extends this line in *Biggest Ideas* to cosmology. The universe began in a state of extremely low entropy — high order, but also high symmetry. Everything was equal, uniform, undifferentiated. The history of the universe is the story of continuous symmetry breaking: first the fundamental forces splitting apart, then matter clumping, then stars forming, then planets, then life. Each step is a transition from potentiality to actuality — from more possibilities to fewer possibilities but more structure.

Heraclitus would recognise it: the world is an eternal becoming, not a static being. And Parmenides would be uneasy: the fundamental laws (his unchangeable Being) are still there, but the reality emerging from them is in constant motion. Aristotle's synthesis — potentiality and actuality as two modes of existence — turns out, after 2,400 years, to have the structure that modern physics needed.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
It is the deepest connection in the entire vault: a line running from the oldest philosophical question (how is change possible?) through modern quantum mechanics (superposition and measurement) to cosmology (symmetry breaking and the structure of the universe). It shows that philosophy and physics are not two separate disciplines but two languages for the same problem: how does possibility become reality?

## Open vragen
- Is quantum collapse a form of symmetry breaking? Some interpretations (decoherence, many-worlds) suggest yes — but there is no consensus.
- Did Aristotle have a point that modern physics misses? His *potentiality* is a property of the thing itself, not of our description. Quantum mechanics is ambiguous on this question — is the wave function a property of reality or of our knowledge?
- If the universe is a series of symmetry breakings, was the original symmetric state "more real" or "less real" than the broken state we inhabit?
