# Epicurus and the longing for tranquility

## Core
Epicurus taught that the highest good is not pleasure in the ordinary sense but *ataraxia* — peace of mind, the absence of fear and pain. Happiness is not the pursuit of pleasures but the elimination of unnecessary desires.

## Explanation
Epicurus (341–270 BCE) is often misunderstood as a hedonist in the modern sense — someone advocating excessive eating, drinking, and partying. The opposite is true. He lived austerely, ate mostly bread and water, and considered a piece of cheese a luxury. His point was not that you should pursue everything pleasant, but that you should learn to distinguish which desires are natural and necessary (food, shelter, friendship), which are natural but not necessary (luxury food, comfort), and which are neither natural nor necessary (fame, power, wealth). The last category is the source of nearly all unhappiness.

Philosophically, Epicurus built on [[Democritus and atomism|Democritus]]: everything consists of atoms and void, the soul is material and perishes with the body, and there is no afterlife. That sounds bleak, but Epicurus turns it around: precisely because death is nothing — "when we are, death is not; when death is, we are not" — we need not fear it. And precisely because no divine punishments await, we can free ourselves from anxiety and focus on what really matters: tranquility, friendship, and the simple pleasure of existence.

Russell sets Epicurus against the [[Stoicism — what you can and cannot control|Stoics]]: both seek peace of mind, but the Stoic finds it in accepting fate, while the Epicurean withdraws from public life into a community of friends ("the Garden"). Russell respects Epicurus's honesty but finds his philosophy too withdrawn — there is no place in it for public engagement or political courage.

Yet Epicurus's influence on the modern world is greater than often acknowledged. His materialism (no supernatural explanations needed) is a precursor of the scientific attitude. And his focus on the elimination of unnecessary suffering — rather than the pursuit of positive pleasures — sounds strikingly modern in light of psychology.

## Why this matters
The question "which desires are truly mine, and which have been imposed on me?" is more current than ever in an age of advertising, social media, and constant stimulation. Epicurus forces you to ask what you really need — and the answer is surprisingly little.

## Open questions
- Is the distinction between natural and unnatural desires sustainable? Who decides what is "natural"?
- How does Epicurus's atomism relate to modern particle physics? He took it from Democritus but added the *clinamen* — a random deviation of atoms that makes free will possible. Is that a precursor of quantum indeterminacy?
