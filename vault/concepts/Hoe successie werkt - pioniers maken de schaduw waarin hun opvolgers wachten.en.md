# How succession works: pioneers create the shade in which their successors wait

## Core
Pioneers win the race for light in a new open patch — but precisely because of that they lose in the long run. Their own seedlings cannot grow in their own shade, while climax species such as beech and oak are shade-tolerant and patiently wait beneath the pioneer canopy. The pioneer literally creates the environment in which its successor takes over. No evolutionary plan, simply the consequence of two opposing light adaptations that follow each other in time.

## Explanation
At first glance succession seems a puzzle. If fast pioneers — birch, alder, poplar, willow — win the race for light in an open patch, how is it that after 50-150 years they are everywhere replaced by slow climax species? Do the oaks and beeches suddenly have a growth spurt? The answer is the opposite: they win not by growing faster, but by waiting in a way that pioneers cannot.

The difference lies in **shade tolerance**. Pioneers have light-hungry seedlings. A birch seed that falls on the floor of a closed birch wood does not germinate, or grows into nothing. Birch literally creates an environment in which it cannot replace itself. The same goes for poplar, willow and alder: they need open, sunny patches to grow.

Beech and oak work the opposite way. Their seedlings are shade-tolerant — they can survive in the deep shade of an existing forest, often for decades in a kind of holding pattern. A young beech can spend twenty years under a birch canopy, growing slowly on the little filtered light that seeps through. It does little, but it survives, and it waits. When an old birch falls and a gap opens in the canopy, the young beech shoots up and fills the place. The pioneer creates the shade in which its successor patiently waits.

Three mechanisms together let slow species eventually win.

**Leaf type.** Climax species have leaves that photosynthesise efficiently at low light. Beech leaves are thin, dark, with much chlorophyll per surface — optimised for the 1-5% of full sunlight that filters through a canopy. Pioneer leaves are optimised for full sunlight and fail at such low intensity. On the forest floor shade leaves always win.

**Lifespan.** A poplar rarely lives beyond 80 years; a birch often not 100. A beech lives 200-400 years, an oak 300-800. Even if pioneers dominate the first decades, they are biologically programmed to die relatively young. Their fast metabolism, thin trunks, and lack of heavy defensive compounds (see [[Arms race in the leaf litter - how plants slow their own decay|the arms race in the leaf litter]]) make them vulnerable to fungi, insects, and storms. Climax species, with their lignin, tannins and thick bark, are built for long-term stress. Give the system enough time — more than a human lifetime — and the balance tips on its own.

**Seed bank and patience.** Beeches produce mast — periods in which they drop acorns or beech nuts en masse, often every 3-5 years. Acorns can survive years in the soil before germinating. An open patch in a pioneer forest is rapidly filled by pioneer seedlings, but in the soil a stock of climax-species seeds is already waiting for the right moment. Pioneers seed on the wind and must constantly find new open patches; climax species invest in seeds that can wait patiently in the same place.

The full pattern, on timescales of centuries, runs as follows: after a disturbance (storm, fire, clear-cut), herbaceous pioneers colonise the open ground in years 1-5. Between years 5 and 30, woody pioneers shoot up — birch, willow, alder — and close the canopy. Beneath their shade, seeds of slow species germinate between years 20 and 80, remaining small for years. Between years 50 and 150, pioneers begin to die, light gaps open, and the waiting climax species shoot through them. After 150-300 years, climax species dominate the canopy. Beneath them no new pioneers germinate — too little light — but new beech and oak do, from seeds of the current canopy. The forest has stabilised itself and can remain so for centuries, until the next disturbance restarts the cycle.

Not all species are either pioneer or climax. Maple and lime sit somewhere in the middle — they tolerate some shade but grow faster than beech and oak. In the natural succession of a Dutch deciduous forest you often get layering: birch and poplar in year 30, maple and lime dominant around year 80, and beech and oak only after a century or more. Every level of shade tolerance has its own phase.

## Why this matters
Succession is not a race in which the fastest wins. It is a kind of ecological "relay" — no evolutionary plan, simply the consequence of light-hungry plants being unable to tolerate their own shade, while shade-tolerant plants have patience as their main weapon. Pioneers and climax species are each other's enemies in the short term (competing for light in a specific place) but each other's partners in the long term. Without pioneers, a disturbed forest would remain bare or erode. Without climax species, a forest would never become a deep, stable ecosystem. The birches you now see in a thirty-year-old wood are dying to make room for the beeches they themselves sheltered when those were small seedlings. This also explains why modern, regularly disturbed landscapes are permanently dominated by pioneers — climax species simply do not get enough time. A 400-year-old beech wood has become so rare in Western Europe that we have almost forgotten it as a reference image of what a forest really looks like.

## Open questions
- How long can a shade-tolerant seedling remain in holding pattern before it dies? Beech seedlings apparently survive twenty to thirty years under dense canopy — what are the physiological limits?
- In an era of climate change and frequent disturbance: can climax forests still establish at all, or will we remain perpetually in pioneer phases?
- Does the same dynamic operate in tropical rainforests, or is the mechanism there fundamentally different due to constant temperature and high biodiversity?
