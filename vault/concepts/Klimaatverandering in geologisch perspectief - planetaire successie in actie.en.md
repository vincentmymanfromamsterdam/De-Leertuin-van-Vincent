# Climate change in geological perspective: planetary succession in action

## Core
The current climate change is biologically a planetary disturbance, with the same dynamics as a fallen tree in a forest — but on the scale of a continent and at a pace that is geologically unprecedented. In the short term r-strategists, generalists and pioneers win. In the long term — over millions of years — new K-species arise that have adapted to the new conditions. What lies between those two phases is an extinction pulse that we happen to be the cause of.

## Explanation
A disturbance in a forest follows a recognisable pattern: an open patch, fast pioneers, shade-tolerant successors, and after a few centuries a restored balance (see [[How succession works - pioneers create the shade in which their successors wait|how succession works]]). The same mechanisms operate at larger scale — only what takes 150 years in a garden can take millions of years on a planetary scale.

On a timescale of a century we see exactly what succession theory predicts. r-strategists — species with fast reproduction, short generation time, many offspring — adapt genetically faster to changing conditions. Not because individuals learn, but because every generation undergoes selection, and r-strategists have more generations per century. A fruit fly has thirty generations a year; an elephant takes twenty years for one. Under rapid climate change that difference is decisive.

What we are already measuring: Mediterranean insects now live in the Netherlands, some tropical species reach southern Europe. Birch forests in Scandinavia are visibly moving northwards while oak forests lag behind. Generalist species such as crows, foxes and rats are doing excellently; specialists such as alpine flora and coral reefs are collapsing. The [[Phenology]] of thousands of species is shifting at a pace their symbiotic partners cannot match. It is essentially the same pattern as any disturbance, only on planetary scale.

**What makes this disturbance unique is its speed.** Every previous mass extinction in Earth's history followed a comparable pattern — a disturbance, a phase of r-dominance, and afterwards millions of years in which new ecosystems built themselves up. The Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event (66 million years ago, the dinosaurs) took about ten million years for full recovery; the Permian-Triassic extinction 252 million years ago — the largest ever — needed thirty million years. But the current change is different. The CO₂ level has risen in 200 years by an amount that nature normally needs 10,000 to 100,000 years for. For many large species — trees that need fifty years to reproduce, mammals with long generation times — this is too fast to allow genetic adaptation. It is not the r-strategists losing; the K-strategists simply cannot keep up.

In geological perspective three phases can be distinguished:

**Phase 1 — now to around 2200 (the pulse).** Rapid r-dominance. Specialists go extinct, generalists and pioneers take over. Some ecosystems — coral reefs, glacier-edge habitats, alpine meadows — probably disappear almost entirely. Ecosystems impoverish strongly in species richness but keep functioning in simplified form. Comparable to the herbaceous stage after a storm in a forest: the place is occupied, but by a fraction of the original diversity.

**Phase 2 — 200 to 10,000 years.** Climate stabilisation, provided CO₂ emissions stop or are reversed. r-strategists remain dominant, but gradual selection pressure begins to favour new variants. Climate zones have permanently shifted. Species that survive this phase begin to specialise. This is the phase of woody pioneers in succession terms — more structure, still limited biodiversity.

**Phase 3 — 100,000 to 10 million years.** True radiation and recovery. New K-species arise, specialised to the new conditions. What was once a tropical zone may, after five million years, have its own set of endemic trees again — only not the same ones as now. New niches are discovered, new symbioses arise. The ecosystem is restored in complexity, but the players are different. The Earth has done this before; it will do it again.

The crucial difference from natural geological disturbances: during phase 1 perhaps 30-50% of all current species disappear, and that gene pool is gone forever. Phase 3 ecosystems are not "restored from" the old ones — they are reinvented from what remained. That is how the Earth has always worked, but the loss of unique genetic history is permanent.

A few nuances to correct the simple dichotomy "r wins, K loses". Not all K-species go under — species with large ranges, phenotypic plasticity or social learning can adapt relatively quickly. And not all r-strategists win — some (honeybees, many insects) are under heavy pressure because their high mortality crosses a threshold. The prediction works statistically, not deterministically.

And then there is humanity — a K-strategist with long generation time that has spread extremely fast and adapts via culture rather than genes. Our intervention (protection, reintroduction, climate corridors) can partly modify the selection pressure other species are under. Whether that is enough to prevent large extinction is an open question.

## Why this matters
What we are now experiencing is not succession as in a garden, but succession on a planetary scale — and we happen to be in the pioneer phase, not as participants but as causes. The insight from ordinary succession theory gives a sobering prediction: r-strategists temporarily dominate, but only millions of years later do new stable ecosystems arise. We are therefore literally living in the "herbaceous phase" after a disturbance — only the disturbance is planetary and the recovery time is geological. That does not change how severe the extinction wave is, but it places it in a pattern the Earth has undergone five times before. We are not the first disturbance; we are only the first that is conscious of what it is causing. Whether that consciousness makes us act differently is precisely what the coming century will determine.

## Open questions
- Which current species will survive the pulse and serve as "founder species" for the new ecosystems of millions of years from now?
- Can human intervention — assisted migration, climate corridors, targeted reintroduction — accelerate the selection rate of K-strategists enough to prevent mass extinction?
- Is the sixth extinction quantitatively comparable to earlier ones, or is its speed so extreme that it belongs in its own category?
