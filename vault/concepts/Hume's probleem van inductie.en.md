# Hume's problem of induction

## Core question
Why are we entitled to conclude, from the fact that the sun has risen every day so far, that it will rise tomorrow? Hume's answer: we aren't — not logically, anyway.

## The problem
Induction is the step from *observed patterns* to *general laws*. We see water boil at 100°C time after time and conclude it *always* will. But that step is not deductively valid. There is no logical contradiction in a world where water suddenly starts boiling at 80°C tomorrow. Hume argued that we can't justify induction *empirically* either — every attempt uses induction itself ("induction has worked well so far, therefore..."), which is circular.

Consequence: our entire empirical knowledge — physics, medicine, daily life — rests on a *custom*, not on reason. We expect the future to resemble the past because our minds are built that way, not because we can prove it should.

## Russell's position
Russell considered this one of the most important problems in philosophy and took it seriously his entire life. In *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912) he argues we must accept the *principle of induction* as an axiom — unprovable but indispensable for science. Not a comfortable position, but an honest one.

## Why this matters
The problem has never been solved; it has only been *worked around*. Karl Popper chose falsification over verification: theories are not confirmed by instances but tested by attempts at refutation. That displaces the problem — falsification itself still assumes the laws of today will hold tomorrow. The honest stance for a modern empiricist is: we trust induction without a foundation, and that is the best we have for now.
