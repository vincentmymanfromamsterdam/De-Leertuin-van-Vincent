# Hume's problem of induction

## Core
We assume the sun will rise tomorrow because it has always done so. But that step — from "until now" to "always" — is logically unprovable. Hume showed that the entire edifice of empirical knowledge rests on a habit of mind, not on reason.

## Explanation
David Hume published *A Treatise of Human Nature* in 1739, at twenty-eight. By his own account, the book "fell dead-born from the press" — and only decades later did it become clear what he had really said. He had pulled the foundation of science out from under science.

The argument is simple, and that is what makes it uncomfortable. Induction is the step from *observed patterns* to *general laws*. We watch water boil at 100°C time after time and conclude it *always* will. But that conclusion is not deductively valid. There is no logical contradiction in a world where water suddenly starts boiling at 80°C tomorrow. Hume then showed that we cannot justify induction *empirically* either — every attempt uses induction itself ("induction has worked well so far, so it will keep working"). That is circular.

What remains? According to Hume: *custom*. We expect the future to resemble the past because our minds are built that way, not because we can prove it should. That holds for physics, for medicine, and for the simple fact that you expect your legs to still hold you up tomorrow morning.

Russell considered this one of the most important problems in philosophy and took it seriously his entire life. In *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912) he argues we must accept the *principle of induction* as an axiom — unprovable but indispensable for science. Not a comfortable position, but an honest one.

[[Karl Popper]] chose a different route in the twentieth century: not verification but *falsification*. Theories are not confirmed by instances but tested by attempts at refutation. That displaces the problem — falsification still assumes the laws of today will hold tomorrow. The problem has never been solved, only worked around.

## Why this matters
The uncomfortable consequence is that our entire scientific enterprise — nuclear physics, medicine, climate models — ultimately rests on something unprovable. That does not make science invalid, but it does make it more fragile than many people think. Hume's point is not a scepticism that science should fight, but a stance of honesty about what we are actually doing. We trust induction without a foundation, and that is the best we have for now.

## Open questions
- Is Russell's solution — accepting induction as an axiom — a real solution or a disguised capitulation?
- How does the problem of induction relate to modern machine learning, which is essentially automated induction?
