# The seasonal rhythm of insects in your garden

## Kern
Insects don't appear randomly throughout the year but follow a predictable seasonal pattern directly linked to temperature, day length and flowering — and understanding that pattern changes how you garden.

## Uitleg
The insect year begins earlier than most people think. Already in February, on the first sunny days against a sheltered wall, overwintering female bumblebees and tortoiseshell butterflies appear. They desperately need nectar after months without food — hence the importance of early spring bloomers.

March-April is the start-up phase. Solitary bees (mason bees, mining bees) are now most active. They nest in hollow stems, wall crevices and bare ground. Unlike honeybees, they live alone — each female builds her own nest and lays her own eggs. They are exceptionally effective pollinators of fruit trees, better than honeybees because they are messier: they spill more pollen.

May-June is the peak. Insect life explodes: ladybirds hunt aphids, hoverflies pollinate while their larvae eat aphids, butterflies appear on buddleia and hemp-agrimony. This is the moment the entire food chain turns — insect-eating birds (great tits, flycatchers) now feed their young with thousands of caterpillars per day.

July-August shifts the picture. Many spring species are past their peak, but moths, grasshoppers and dragonflies increase. Wild bee activity shifts to late bloomers: ivy, heather, late-flowering composites.

September-October is the wind-down. The last generation of butterflies (red admiral, peacock) fuels up on overripe fruit and ivy blossom. Bumblebee queens seek hibernation spots. Most insects die, but their eggs, pupae or overwintering adults wait for the next spring.

What this means for garden management: continuity of bloom is more important than peaks. A garden that only explodes in June starves insects in March and October. The ideal is a continuous flowering buffet from February to November, which aligns precisely with the concept of ecological succession: different plant layers succeeding each other in bloom.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
Insects are the invisible engine of your garden. They pollinate your fruit trees, control pest insects, feed the birds and process organic matter. Understanding the seasonal rhythm turns "insects" from something that happens to you into something you can facilitate.
