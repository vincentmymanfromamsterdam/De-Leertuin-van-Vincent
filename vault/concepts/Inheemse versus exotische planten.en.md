# Native versus exotic plants

## Kern
A native hawthorn supports over 200 insect species. An exotic conifer supports almost none. That difference explains why plant choice is the single most decisive factor for biodiversity in your garden.

## Uitleg
Native plants — species that occur naturally in a region and have co-evolved with local insect life over thousands of years — form the basis of the food web. Caterpillars of most butterfly species can only survive on specific native host plants. Brimstone butterfly eggs are laid exclusively on alder buckthorn and purging buckthorn. Orange-tip caterpillars eat only cuckooflower and garlic mustard. No native host plant = no caterpillar = no butterfly = less food for insect-eating birds.

Exotic plants — rhododendron, cherry laurel, bamboo, many popular garden centre plants — are largely food deserts for local insect life. They look green but function ecologically like plastic. A cherry laurel hedge is the biodiversity equivalent of a wooden fence.

This doesn't mean every exotic is bad. Some garden plants from Southern Europe or Asia offer nectar to generalist pollinators (lavender, Verbena bonariensis). But they lack the deep co-evolutionary relationships that native plants have: the specific caterpillars, the solitary bees attuned to exactly that flower, the fungi that partner with those roots.

Practical rule of thumb: aim for at least 70% native planting as a foundation, supplemented with selected exotics that add nectar value during periods when native species offer little bloom (especially late summer and autumn).

Strong native choices for a garden in the Gooi region: hawthorn (shrub/tree, 200+ insect species), blackthorn (early bloom, berries for birds), wild privet (pollinators, berries), ivy (indispensable autumn bloomer for bumblebees and butterflies), wild honeysuckle (moths), pollarded willow (early pollen source).

## Waarom het ertoe doet
Plant choice is the most impactful decision you can make in your garden. It's not the nest boxes, not the insect hotel, not the pond — it's what's in the ground. One hawthorn does more ecologically than ten insect hotels.

## Open vragen
- Does climate change shift the definition of "native"? Species currently found in Belgium but not the Netherlands are moving northward.
- Is a garden with 100% native plants realistic in an urban environment, or is a mix the best aesthetic and ecological compromise?
