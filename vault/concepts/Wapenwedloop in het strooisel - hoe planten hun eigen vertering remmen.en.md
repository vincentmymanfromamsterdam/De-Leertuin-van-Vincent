# Arms race in the leaf litter: how plants slow their own decay

## Core
Trees and mosses actively invest in their own indigestibility. Tannins, lignin, and polyphenols are not accidental compounds but evolutionary weapons against fungi, bacteria, and insects — weapons that keep working long after the leaf has fallen. What is irritating in your compost heap shapes entire biomes at landscape scale: forest understorey, raised bog, and the coal seams now reshaping our atmosphere.

## Explanation
A living plant has a problem: it is rooted in place and cannot run from whoever wants to eat it. Its defence is chemical. Three classes of compound dominate that defence, and all three remain active long after leaf-fall — they restrain not only the living attacker but also the decomposer.

**Tannins** are tanning agents — the same class of compounds that hardens leather and makes tea bitter. In beech and oak leaves they reach concentrations of several percent of dry weight. For a caterpillar they make the leaf indigestible; for microbes they bind enzymes and proteins so that decay nearly stalls. A beech leaf that falls in September lies recognisably on the forest floor two Septembers later. That is no accident: the tree spends energy producing those inhibitors, and the evolutionary return is a thick litter layer that smothers competitors. A beech wood characteristically has very little undergrowth.

**Lignin** is the woody polymer that gives plants their structural strength. It is structural, not defensive in origin, but it became evolutionarily one of the most important decay-barriers ever. Only fungi — and even then only white-rot and brown-rot basidiomycetes — can break it down effectively, using peroxidase enzymes that operate via highly reactive oxygen radicals. In the Carboniferous period, 360-300 million years ago, trees evolved lignin faster than fungi could decompose it. Trunks lay where they fell, sometimes for 60 million years. That is how our coal seams formed — fossilised, undecomposed wood. Only when fungi eventually evolved the right enzymes did that era end and wood become normal recyclable material. But the race is not over: trees keep producing lignin, fungi keep refining their decomposition arsenal.

**Moss** took a different route. Mosses never evolved lignin, but instead produce polyphenols and sphagnan — tough polysaccharides that actively inhibit microbial activity. Peat moss (*Sphagnum*) goes further and becomes an active niche engineer: it secretes hydrogen ions, actively acidifying its environment (pH 3-4), holds extreme amounts of water, and excludes oxygen. No single decomposer can simultaneously cope with dry, nutrient-poor, acidic, and anaerobic conditions. The moss literally creates the conditions in which it itself wins. Raised bogs — Bargerveen, Fochteloërveen in the Netherlands — are metres-thick peat, accumulated over thousands of years of moss making itself indigestible. Worldwide, peatlands store more carbon than all the forests combined.

Here lies the difference between lignin and moss chemistry. Lignin was a one-off, passive evolutionary innovation that was eventually broken. Moss's indigestibility, by contrast, is a coevolutionary equilibrium: as soon as a microbe gets better at moss-decay, the moss is under selection pressure to refine its inhibitors. It is a real arms race, and in cold, wet, acidic places the moss consistently wins. That is why peat remains peat.

## Why this matters
The everyday observation — "my beech leaves don't compost, and the moss certainly doesn't" — is a window onto a more fundamental principle. The carbon cycle of the Earth is determined not only by what grows, but equally by what does *not* decay. Coal, natural gas, peat, forest humus: all storage banks that exist thanks to plant chemical defences that slow or halt decomposition. Our atmosphere has been shaped in part by hundreds of millions of years of plants investing in indigestibility. And when we burn coal and peat, we unspool that storage over decades rather than millennia — we reverse an evolutionary process that was actually letting our biosphere lock away carbon for climatic stability. Your compost heap shows the micro-version of the same physics.

## Open questions
- Does the arms race ever stop, or does it keep evolving as long as plants and decomposers exist?
- Which modern ecosystems resemble the Carboniferous — places where decay lags structurally behind production and carbon actively accumulates?
- Does climate change shift the balance between plant defence and microbial decomposition? Warmer soils decompose faster — is loss of peat-stored carbon a feedback mechanism?
