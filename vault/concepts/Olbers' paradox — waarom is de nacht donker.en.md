# Olbers' paradox — why is the night dark?

## Core
If the universe is infinitely large and contains stars everywhere, then every line of sight in the sky should eventually end on a star. The night sky should not be dark but blindingly bright. The fact that it isn't tells us something profound about the nature of the universe.

## Explanation
The paradox is older than Olbers (who reformulated it in 1823) — Kepler and Halley already wrestled with it. The reasoning is simple: if you stand in an infinite forest, you will eventually see a tree in every direction, no matter how far away. In the same way, in an infinite universe full of stars you should see a stellar surface in every direction. The whole sky should glow like the surface of the sun.

Several solutions have been proposed — dust absorbing light, stars too faint — but they don't work. Dust that absorbs light heats up itself and starts to radiate. And the paradox is not about individual stars but about the total amount of light.

The real solution combines two cosmological facts. First: the universe is not infinitely old. It is around 13.8 billion years old ([[Big Bang]]), meaning that light from stars further than 13.8 billion light-years away has not yet reached us. There is a horizon. Second: [[Redshift]] shifts light from distant stars toward longer wavelengths, ultimately well beyond the visible spectrum. Distant stars are not only far away but have become invisible.

The dark night sky is thus not a triviality but cosmological evidence: the universe had a beginning, and it is expanding. If either of those two things were not true, the night would not be dark.

Al-Khalili treats this in *Paradox* as one of the nine "greatest enigmas" and emphasises that it is a paradox that has been solved — but that the solution is more surprising than the problem.

## Why this matters
It is one of the finest examples of how an everyday observation (the night is dark) carries a profound cosmological implication. You don't need to look at the stars to do cosmology — you only have to ask why you don't see them *everywhere*.

## Open questions
- If the universe keeps expanding forever, will the night gradually grow darker as more galaxies disappear beyond our horizon?
- Is there an analogous argument for gravity — a "gravitational Olbers' paradox"?
