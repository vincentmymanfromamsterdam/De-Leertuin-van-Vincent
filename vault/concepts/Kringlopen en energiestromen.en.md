# Cycles and energy flows — why the banana comes back but the heat does not

## Kern
In nature, matter cycles — the same carbon atoms travel from air to leaf to fruit to compost to soil to root and back to leaf. But energy flows, in one direction, from the Sun into space. Cycles only exist because that one-way flow drives them.

## Uitleg
Take the cycle of a banana. The banana tree captures sunlight through photosynthesis and uses it to build CO₂ from the air and water from the soil into sugars — the flesh of the banana. You eat the banana, toss the peel into the bokashi or the compost heap. Micro-organisms and earthworms break the peel down into humus and minerals. Those nutrients return via the soil food web and mycorrhizal networks to the tree's roots. The tree builds a new banana. The cycle is complete.

But there is a difference between what goes round and what does not. *Matter* cycles: the carbon atoms in the new banana are literally the same atoms that were once in the old peel, and before that in the air, and before that perhaps in a dinosaur. Atoms are indestructible (at least on biological scales) and are recycled endlessly.

*Energy* does not cycle. At every step in the loop, part of the energy is lost as heat — infrared radiation that the Earth emits into cold space. The tree captures high-energy solar photons and converts them into chemical energy (sugars). Your body burns those sugars and releases heat. The compost heap breaks down the peel and releases heat. At each step, usable energy is converted into unusable heat. That is entropy increase: not loss of energy (the total amount is conserved) but loss of *usability*.

The cycle is therefore not a perpetual motion machine. It is a water mill. The water (matter) goes round and round, but the river (energy from the Sun) drives the wheel. Stop the river — the Sun goes out — and the wheel stops. Within one generation of trees, photosynthesis ceases, the compost is exhausted, the cycle collapses.

This is the difference between the first and second laws of thermodynamics in action. The first law says: energy is conserved, nothing is lost. The second law says: with every conversion, the energy becomes a little less usable. The cycling of matter obeys the first law. The one-way flow of energy obeys the second.

The Sun itself is not an eternal source either. It burns hydrogen into helium through nuclear fusion — a process that will stop in roughly 5 billion years when the fuel runs out. The river driving all cycles on Earth is finite. And the energy the Sun radiates is eventually stretched to ever longer wavelengths by the expansion of the universe, until it is so thinly spread that nothing can be done with it.

Follow the line all the way through and you see the full cascade: the Big Bang provided the low-entropy initial condition. Gravity formed stars. Stars produce the energy flow that drives cycles on planets. Those cycles create local order — trees, bananas, compost heaps, garden ecosystems, you — at the cost of global entropy increase. And eventually, on a timescale that defies imagination, the universe reaches maximum entropy and all cycles, all flows, all processes stop.

But that is trillions of years away. Today the Sun shines, the banana grows, and the cycle turns. And every time you toss a banana peel on the compost heap, you participate in a process as old as life itself — recycling matter while the Sun pays the energy bill.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
It changes how you look at your garden. A composter who sees only cycles thinks the system sustains itself. A composter who also sees the energy flow understands that everything — from the compost heap to the rainforest to the entire biosphere — depends on one source: the Sun. And that source is finite, not on human timescales, but on cosmic ones.

It also connects the three domains of the vault in the most direct way. Philosophy asks: where does everything come from (Arche)? Cosmology answers: from a low-entropy initial condition, driven by gravity and nuclear fusion. Nature shows: and this is what that looks like at the scale of a back garden — a banana peel returning as a banana, powered by starlight.

## Open vragen
- If all energy on Earth ultimately comes from the Sun, how many "sunlight steps" are there between a photon and the banana on your plate?
- Geothermal energy is an exception — it comes from radioactive decay in the Earth's core, not from the Sun. How large is the contribution of geothermal energy to biological cycles compared to sunlight?
- Is it useful to design a garden as the most efficient "solar energy catcher" possible — maximum photosynthesis per square metre?
