# The arrow of time

## Core
Eggs break, coffee cools, memories are about the past and not the future. But the fundamental laws of physics are nearly all time-symmetric — they work just as well backwards as forwards. So where does the arrow we feel every second come from?

## Explanation
Newton's mechanics, Maxwell's electromagnetism, Schrödinger's equation, Einstein's field equations — play the motions in a film backwards, and the physics still holds. The laws themselves have no preference for "before" or "after". And yet our experience is brutally asymmetric. We remember yesterday, not tomorrow. History is written, the future is not. How?

The answer, as Ludwig Boltzmann worked it out in the nineteenth century, lies in *entropy*. The second law of thermodynamics states: in a closed system, entropy (a measure of disorder, or more precisely: the number of microscopic configurations that yield the same macroscopic state) tends to increase. A freshly stacked deck has low entropy — only one way to be that ordered. A shuffled deck has high entropy — astronomically many ways to be "shuffled". Shuffling is therefore statistically easy; un-shuffling is statistically impossible.

But — and here it gets interesting — Boltzmann already saw that this only explains the arrow halfway. The law works symmetrically. A low-entropy system evolves toward higher entropy *in both directions in time*, statistically. The most probable path to now is that the past was also chaotic and that our current ordered world is a chance fluctuation. But that clearly isn't right — we have fossils, history books, remains of earlier order.

The modern solution, worked out by [[Sean Carroll]] and the philosopher David Albert among others, is called the Past Hypothesis. Our universe began in a state of extremely low entropy. The [[Big Bang]] was not only hot and dense, it was also strikingly *ordered*. From that starting point, entropy can only go up. The arrow of time in your kitchen — coffee cooling, eggs not unbreaking — is a direct imprint of the initial condition of the entire universe 13.8 billion years ago.

Boltzmann himself was haunted by these problems. He fought with colleagues who rejected his statistical approach and took his own life in 1906. On his gravestone in Vienna stands his formula: *S = k log W*. The simplest equation for the hardest question.

## Why this matters
Time feels obvious. It isn't. The simple daily experience that the past is fixed and the future is open is a *cosmological* fact, not a *local* one. We don't know why the Big Bang had such an incredibly low entropy — that is one of the deepest open questions in physics. But without that one mysterious initial condition, time as we know it would not exist. Every moment you watch a cup of coffee cool, you are unconsciously reminding yourself of the order from which the universe began.

## Open questions
- Why did the Big Bang have such low entropy? Coincidence, necessity, or the consequence of something deeper?
- Are there regions somewhere in a multiverse where the arrow of time runs the other way?
