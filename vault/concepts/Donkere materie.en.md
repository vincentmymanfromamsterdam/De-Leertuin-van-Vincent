# Dark matter

## Kern
Roughly 85% of all matter in the universe is invisible — it emits no light, absorbs no light, and does not interact with electromagnetic radiation. We know it exists because we observe its gravitational effects. What it actually is remains unknown.

## Uitleg
The problem first became apparent in the 1930s when Fritz Zwicky measured the velocities of galaxies in the Coma Cluster. They were moving far too fast to remain bound together — as if there were far more mass than could be seen. Zwicky called it "dunkle Materie." For decades, he was ignored.

In the 1970s, Vera Rubin confirmed the same problem at a different scale. She measured the rotation speeds of stars within spiral galaxies and discovered that stars at the edges rotate just as fast as stars near the centre. That's strange: if only the visible matter were present, outer stars should orbit much more slowly (just as Neptune orbits the Sun more slowly than Mercury). The rotation pattern only made sense if every galaxy is surrounded by an invisible halo of extra mass.

Since then, the evidence has become overwhelming. The cosmic microwave background radiation shows patterns that only match if dark matter exists. The way galaxies bend light from background objects (gravitational lensing) reveals mass that "shouldn't be there." The large-scale structure of the universe — how galaxies are distributed in filaments and clusters with empty voids between them — can only be reproduced in simulations that include dark matter.

What it isn't: it's not black holes, not cold gas clouds, not undetected stars. Those options have been systematically ruled out. It must be something we don't yet know — a new type of particle that interacts with ordinary matter only through gravity.

Carroll emphasises that this is not a minor detail. The matter from which you, the Earth, the Sun and all the stars are made constitutes only 15% of all matter. We are the by-product; dark matter is the main course.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
It is one of the biggest open questions in physics. We have a universe in which we cannot identify 85% of the matter. At the same time, dark matter is essential for the existence of galaxies — without it, gravity would not produce enough clumping to form structures. We exist thanks to something we do not understand.

## Open vragen
- Are there alternative gravity theories (MOND) that make dark matter unnecessary, or do they fail under precise testing?
- If dark matter is a particle, why haven't particle accelerators found it yet?
