# Four forms of climate scepticism: what sceptics actually say

## Core
"Climate sceptic" is not a homogeneous category but a collective term for at least four different positions, each based on different premises and with different scientific status. Anyone who wants to take the debate seriously should distinguish between trend-scepticism (is the Earth actually warming?), attribution-scepticism (is humanity the cause?), impact-scepticism (how severe will it get?), and policy-scepticism (is this policy the right response?). The first two are largely marginalised within mainstream science; the third touches on uncertainties that mainstream researchers also acknowledge; the fourth is not a scientific but a political-economic discussion.

## Explanation
The generic "sceptics don't believe in climate change" hides four different kinds of criticism with very different validity claims. Honest distinction helps to assess the arguments on their own merits.

**Trend-sceptics** dispute that the Earth is substantially warming, or argue that warming falls within natural variation. Main arguments: surface temperature measurements are contaminated by the urban heat island effect (cities warmer, weather stations often in cities); satellite measurements (UAH, RSS) show smaller warming than surface measurements; the "pause" between 1998 and 2014 shows that climate models are wrong; earlier warm periods such as the Medieval Climate Optimum or the Roman Warm Period were possibly warmer than today.

Notable names: Anthony Watts (wattsupwiththat.com), Roy Spencer (climate researcher UAH satellites), John Christy. This position is largely marginalised within mainstream climate science — the warming is empirically so clear that this camp is contradicted in nearly every peer-reviewed publication. What did come from this criticism: better measurement methods, homogenisation of temperature series, and serious corrections for urban influence. The science has done better work because of it, even if the conclusion did not land where the sceptics had hoped.

**Attribution-sceptics** acknowledge that the Earth is warming, but doubt that humans are the main cause. Arguments: the sun varies in activity and can explain much warming (Henrik Svensmark's hypothesis that cosmic rays drive cloud formation); ocean cycles such as PDO and AMO explain multi-decadal variation; CO₂ is a trace gas and human emissions are small compared to natural flux; the correlation between CO₂ and temperature in ice cores shows that CO₂ *follows* warming, not causes it.

Notable names: Henrik Svensmark, Nir Shaviv, Richard Lindzen (MIT emeritus). Lindzen is the weightiest scientific voice in this category — he particularly doubts **climate sensitivity** (how many degrees of warming per doubling of CO₂). Mainstream IPCC says 2.5-4 °C; Lindzen thinks 0.5-1.5 °C through self-regulating mechanisms he calls the "iris effect" — tropical cloud cover that radiates more heat outward as it warms, like an iris adjusting.

The mainstream response is clear: solar variability is measured and can only explain a fraction of observed warming. The ice-core sequence holds for natural cycles (where temperature rises first and CO₂ follows through ocean outgassing), but that does not refute that anthropogenic CO₂ is now the first cause — that is a different causal direction. And the iris effect has been sought repeatedly in observations and not confirmed. Still, this is a scientifically more serious debate than trend-scepticism, because it concerns quantitative uncertainties discussed within scientific literature.

**Impact-sceptics** — often called "lukewarmers" — acknowledge that humans are warming the Earth, but consider the impact overstated. Arguments: doubling CO₂ gives directly about 1 °C of warming, only through water vapour feedbacks does it become more, and those feedbacks are uncertain; adaptation is cheaper than prevention, rich societies adapt easily; CO₂ has positive effects on crop yields (CO₂ fertilisation effect, measured in hundreds of experiments); cold mortality globally exceeds heat mortality, so moderate warming may be net positive; climate models run consistently warmer than observations and overstate sensitivity.

Notable names: Bjorn Lomborg (political scientist, *The Skeptical Environmentalist*), Matt Ridley (journalist), Judith Curry (climate scientist Georgia Tech), Nicholas Lewis (statistician). Curry is particularly interesting because she stepped out of mainstream consensus as an established climate researcher and specifically emphasises the uncertainty ranges. Her argument: IPCC presents too narrow probability ranges and underestimates the possibility of low climate sensitivity. Lewis has co-authored publications with her estimating, based on observed warming so far, that sensitivity lies in the lower part of the IPCC range.

This is scientifically the most serious category, because the arguments are measurable and partly overlap with uncertainties that mainstream climate researchers themselves acknowledge. IPCC ranges for climate sensitivity are wide (1.5-4.5 °C in older reports, 2.5-4 °C in AR6) precisely because this quantity is hard to pin down. The question is whether the uncertainties are large enough to change policy, or whether they fall within the expected range. Mainstream researchers such as Michael Mann and Gavin Schmidt argue that even at the low end of the range, impact is severe and justifies action; lukewarmers argue that low sensitivity plus adaptation possibilities make aggressive mitigation unnecessarily expensive.

**Policy-sceptics** accept climate science but doubt the proposed policy solutions. Arguments: mitigation policy (CO₂ reduction) is more expensive than adaptation; renewable energy is not yet reliable enough to replace fossil; unilateral European measures damage our competitive position without much global effect; climate activism leads to bad technology choices (wind turbines instead of nuclear, biomass as "green"); the poor world needs fossil fuels to become prosperous — climate policy makes the poor pay for the rich's problem.

Notable names: Lomborg (Copenhagen Consensus), Michael Shellenberger (*Apocalypse Never*), Alex Epstein (*The Moral Case for Fossil Fuels*). This position is not climate-science criticism but economic-political criticism. The debates are about choices under uncertainty, not about physics. A nuclear advocate and a wind-power advocate can fully agree on the physics yet fully disagree on the solution.

**Three sources from which sceptical arguments come** are useful to distinguish, because they have different kinds of validity claims.

The first is **peer-reviewed scientific literature**. A minority of researchers publishes criticism of specific aspects of mainstream consensus. Lindzen, Curry, Christy, Spencer, Svensmark and Lewis have academic publications with their arguments. This is the most seriously to be taken source, even if the mainstream often rejects their conclusions. Science thrives on such disagreements — a field in which no one challenges consensus would be unhealthy.

The second is **think tanks and advocacy organisations**. An important funding stream for sceptical arguments comes from institutes such as the Heartland Institute (US), Global Warming Policy Foundation (UK), and historically also from fossil fuel companies (especially ExxonMobil, which had its own climate researchers predicting warming in the 1970s while the company publicly sowed doubt). The role of these organisations is documented in research such as that of science historians Naomi Oreskes and Erik Conway in *Merchants of Doubt* (2010). This is important context — not to dismiss the arguments as "paid", but to understand why some arguments receive more media spread than their scientific status would justify.

The third source is a **broader intellectual tradition of scepticism**. Some sceptics come from a liberal, libertarian or conservative tradition that distrusts large government interventions, model-based predictions, and scientific authority in policy matters. For them, climate scepticism is often connected to scepticism toward other consensus formations — not from bad faith, but from a principled stance toward top-down knowledge claims. This touches on a deeper philosophical debate about the epistemic role of science in democracy, going back to [[Karl Popper|Popper]]'s reflections on science and the open society, and to [[Hume's problem of induction|Hume's problem of induction]] on what we can know about future trends.

**What is scientifically tenable and what is not.** An honest summary of where the arguments stand: nearly certainly wrong by mainstream are the claim that the Earth is not warming (evidence is overwhelming), the claim "CO₂ follows temperature so does not cause it" (holds for natural cycles, not for the anthropogenic situation), and the claim "it is all the sun" (solar activity is measured and explains only a fraction). Substantial uncertainty where serious scientists disagree concerns climate sensitivity (mainstream 2.5-4 °C, some researchers lower), the speed of tipping points, how much water vapour feedback contributes, and regional impact. Legitimate policy debate includes the best mitigation strategy, balance between mitigation and adaptation, cost-benefit over different time horizons, and distributional questions between poor and rich countries.

**Three misconceptions that often muddy the discussion.** The first is that "the science is settled". Mainstream climate science acknowledges more uncertainties than often presented in media — IPCC reports give wide probability ranges, which in the summary for policymakers are often narrowed for readability. Sceptics sometimes rightly point this out. The second is that "all sceptics are paid by oil". Some have had fossil funding; others have not. Lindzen had no fossil ties early in his career and is an MIT physicist with a deviating interpretation. Curry was previously an IPCC contributor. Motivations are diverse. The third is that "climate action means one specific policy". Acknowledging that climate change is real and human-caused does not automatically say which policy is best. A nuclear advocate and a renewables advocate can fully agree on the physics.

## Why this matters
The climate debate in public discussion is often presented as "science versus denial", but that does justice to neither the science nor the scepticism. The most serious sceptical arguments are about **climate sensitivity** and **policy trade-offs**, not about the warming itself. For those who want to follow the scientific debates, the most valuable sceptical voices are those of Judith Curry, Richard Lindzen and Nicholas Lewis — not because they are necessarily right, but because they articulate uncertainties explicitly and argue with data. For policy debates Lomborg and Shellenberger are valuable counter-voices, even if you do not share their conclusions. What stands out is that the debates are strongest when they are specific and technical, and weakest when they drift into generic "alarmism" framing. Real scientific disagreement about feedback loops in tropical cloud cover is interesting; "climate change is a hoax" is not. Distinguishing between the four categories helps to assess the right arguments on the right merits, and prevents the entire debate from sliding into caricature on both sides.

## Open questions
- How would the scientific discussion run if the political implications were smaller? Have some researchers been forced into more caution because scepticism has become politically loaded?
- What historical examples are there of mainstream scientific consensus that eventually proved wrong? And what features did those debates share?
- What is the role of science in democratic policy-making when uncertainty ranges are wide but decisions must be made? Who holds the epistemic authority?

## Further reading
- Naomi Oreskes & Erik Conway, *Merchants of Doubt* (2010) — history of how industry-funded scepticism was organised, from tobacco to climate
- Judith Curry, [Climate Etc. blog](https://judithcurry.com/) — one of the most serious sceptical voices, with technical analyses
- Richard Lindzen, "[Climate Science: Is it currently designed to answer questions?](https://arxiv.org/abs/0809.3762)" (2008) — clear formulation of scientific criticism from an MIT physicist
- Bjorn Lomborg, *False Alarm* (2020) — policy-sceptical analysis, controversial but influential
- Michael Mann, *The New Climate War* (2021) — mainstream climate scientist on how scepticism works as rhetorical strategy
- IPCC AR6, [Working Group I](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg1/) — for those who want to see for themselves how the mainstream presents its uncertainty ranges
