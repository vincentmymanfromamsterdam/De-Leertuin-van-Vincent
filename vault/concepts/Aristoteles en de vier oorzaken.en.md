# Aristotle and the four causes

## Kern
Aristotle argued that you only truly understand something when you know four kinds of causes: what it's made of (material), what structure it has (formal), what set it in motion (efficient), and what it's for (final). The last — purpose — is the most controversial and influential.

## Uitleg
Take an oak table. The material cause is the wood. The formal cause is the design — four legs, a top, certain proportions. The efficient cause is the carpenter who made it. The final cause is the purpose: to place things on, to eat at.

So far this sounds like common sense. But Aristotle applies this framework to nature itself. An acorn grows into an oak — the final cause of the acorn is to become an oak. Rain falls so that plants can grow. Everything in nature has a purpose, a *telos*.

This is teleological thinking: explaining the world through purposes. It stands in direct opposition to Democritus, who explained everything through blind mechanical interactions of atoms — no purpose, no plan, just collisions. Russell observes that the entire history of Western science can be read as a slow victory of Democritus over Aristotle: modern physics and biology are fundamentally mechanistic, not teleological.

But Aristotle's influence was enormous and long-lasting. Through the Arab philosophers (Avicenna, Averroës) and Thomas Aquinas, his thinking dominated medieval philosophy and theology. The "final cause" fitted perfectly with the Christian idea of a purposeful creation.

Russell is ambivalent. He admires Aristotle's systematic mind — his logic, his biology, his political philosophy — but considers teleology an intellectual brake that delayed science for centuries. Only when Galileo and Newton showed that nature can be described without purposes did modern science take off.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
Teleological thinking is deeply human — we are purpose-seeking creatures and project that onto the world. The question of whether nature is truly purposeless (Democritus, modern science) or purposeful (Aristotle) touches the heart of how we relate to the universe. And in biology, the question is subtle: evolution has no purpose, yet produces organisms that *look* purposeful.

## Open vragen
- Is evolutionary biology secretly teleological? We say "eyes are for seeing" — is that Aristotelian thinking in disguise?
- Does the final cause have a comeback in cosmology? Some physicists (Carroll among them) ask why the initial conditions of the universe were precisely such that complexity could arise.
