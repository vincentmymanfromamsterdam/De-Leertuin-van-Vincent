# Noether's theorem

## Kern
Every symmetry in nature corresponds to a conservation law. The fact that the laws of physics work the same today as yesterday gives you conservation of energy. The fact that they work the same here as over there gives you conservation of momentum. This connection — proved by Emmy Noether in 1915 — is one of the deepest insights in all of physics.

## Uitleg
Emmy Noether was a German mathematician who worked at a time when women were not allowed to lecture at universities. Her theorem, published in 1918, connects two seemingly independent domains: symmetry and conservation laws.

Symmetry in physics doesn't mean "left looks like right" but something more abstract: the laws of nature don't change under certain transformations. Three examples:

Time translation symmetry: the laws of physics are the same today as tomorrow. Noether shows this automatically leads to conservation of energy — energy cannot appear from or disappear into nothing.

Space translation symmetry: the laws are the same here as a metre further on. This leads to conservation of momentum.

Rotational symmetry: the laws are the same regardless of which direction you look. This leads to conservation of angular momentum.

Feynman treats energy conservation in Piece 4 of Six Easy Pieces as an empirical fact — "we don't know what energy is, but we know it's conserved." Noether's theorem provides the why: energy is conserved because time is symmetric. If time symmetry were ever broken (if the laws of physics worked differently tomorrow), energy conservation would no longer hold.

Carroll calls Noether's theorem "the most important result in theoretical physics that most people have never heard of." It connects the abstract world of Pythagoras — mathematics as the structure of reality — with the concrete world of measurable quantities.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
It changes how you view conservation laws. They are not arbitrary rules that nature happens to follow, but inevitable consequences of deeper symmetries. And when a symmetry is broken, the associated conservation law disappears — which actually happens in cosmology (the expansion of the universe breaks time symmetry, meaning energy is not strictly conserved on cosmic scales).
