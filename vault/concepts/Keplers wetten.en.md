# Kepler's laws

## Kern
In the early 17th century, Johannes Kepler discovered that planets do not move in perfect circles around the Sun, but in ellipses — and formulated three laws that describe their motion exactly.

## Uitleg
For two thousand years, the idea dominated that celestial motions had to be circular. The circle was the "perfect" shape — Plato and Aristotle demanded it, Pythagoras would have expected it, and Copernicus still clung to it when he placed the Sun at the centre. But the observations never quite matched. Endless corrections (epicycles — circles upon circles) were needed to make the data fit.

Kepler, working with Tycho Brahe's exceptionally precise observations, did what no one before him had dared: he abandoned the circle. After years of calculations, he discovered that Mars's orbit is an ellipse with the Sun at one focus. That became his first law.

His second law describes how fast a planet moves: closer to the Sun it speeds up, farther away it slows down, in such a way that the line from Sun to planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times.

His third law connects orbital period to distance: the square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the average distance from the Sun. Jupiter, five times farther than Earth, takes not five but nearly twelve years to complete an orbit.

Sagan describes in *Cosmos* how painful this process was for Kepler. He was himself a mystic who believed in the harmony of the spheres — he wanted planetary orbits to fit into a scheme of nested Platonic solids. But the data forced him to abandon his own conviction. That is science at its strongest: the willingness to drop a beautiful idea when the facts don't support it.

Feynman cites Kepler's laws as the empirical foundation upon which Newton built his theory of gravity. Newton showed that all three of Kepler's laws follow automatically from one simple assumption: gravity decreases with the square of the distance.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
This is the moment when aesthetics lost to data. Two thousand years of circle dogma were broken by someone who had the courage to take measurements more seriously than tradition. That makes Kepler not just an astronomer but a hero of the scientific method.

## Open vragen
- Why were ellipses so hard to accept? Is there a psychological explanation for the human preference for symmetrical shapes?
- Kepler's laws describe what happens, but not why. The "why" only came with Newton. Is there a modern parallel — phenomena we describe but cannot explain?
