# Phase transitions of the early universe: a cascade of broken symmetries

## Core
The universe cooled from extreme temperatures to what it is now — and at each critical temperature its character changed fundamentally. Forces split apart, particles acquired mass, quarks became confined inside protons. These were not isolated events but instances of the same mathematical pattern: spontaneous symmetry breaking, the same physics you see every day when water freezes or iron loses its magnetism. And in principle every breaking is reversible — though humanity would need a galaxy-sized accelerator to make that real for most of them.

## Explanation
The first second after the Big Bang is not a smooth cooling curve but a series of sharp transitions, each at its own critical temperature. A uniform high-energy state repeatedly "froze" into an ordered lower-energy state, releasing energy and giving rise to structure that did not exist before. What we observe today as "the laws of nature" are the frozen remains of those transitions.

**The cascade.**

| Time | Temperature | Transition |
|---|---|---|
| 10⁻⁴³ s | 10³² K | Gravity splits off from the other forces (Planck transition) |
| 10⁻³⁶ s | 10²⁸ K | Strong nuclear force splits off, end of GUT epoch, possibly triggers inflation |
| 10⁻³² s | 10²⁷ K | End of inflation, reheating: inflaton field decays into particles |
| 10⁻¹² s | 10¹⁵ K | Higgs field freezes: particles gain mass, electroweak force splits |
| 10⁻⁶ s | 10¹³ K | Chiral symmetry breaking: quarks become confined in protons and neutrons |
| 380,000 yr | 3,000 K | Recombination: electrons capture nuclei, universe becomes transparent |

Each of these transitions follows the same mathematical pattern that works at the [[The Curie point|Curie point]]. Above a critical temperature a system is rotationally symmetric: all directions equivalent, no preference. Below that temperature the system "chooses" one configuration spontaneously — which configuration does not matter, but once chosen it is locked in. In iron, electron spins choose an alignment direction. In the Higgs field, the universe chose a ground state in which particles have mass. In the chiral transition, quarks chose to clump together in threes inside protons and neutrons rather than roam freely. One mathematical motif, many physical guises.

**The remarkable thing about this story** is that the laws of nature do not "know" which configuration to choose. The symmetry is *spontaneously* broken — by minuscule quantum fluctuations amplified into a macroscopic choice. Different regions of the universe could in principle have made different choices, and some inflation models predict that distant regions of the universe actually made different choices. We live in one specific configuration because that is how it happened to fall out when our patch cooled.

**Reversible — in theory.**

Here comes a beautiful piece of mathematical honesty: all these phase transitions are in principle **reversible**. The same equations that describe how a symmetry breaks during cooling also describe how it is restored during heating. At the Curie point you see this every day in a laboratory: heat a magnet above 770 °C, the magnetism disappears; cool it down, the magnetism returns. The symmetry is repeatedly "glued and broken" without any fundamental difficulty.

The question is therefore not whether we can restore other cosmic phase transitions, but at what temperature. And there it gets sobering. The Curie transition works at energies of about 0.1 electron-volts per particle. The electroweak transition requires 100 billion electron-volts — a factor of 10¹² higher. The GUT transition another factor of 10¹⁴ above that. And the Planck scale another factor of 10³ further.

**What we have done.** The one case where we have actually restored a cosmic symmetry temporarily is at **quark-gluon plasma**. In the Relativistic Heavy Ion Collider at Brookhaven and at ALICE in CERN, heavy ions (lead, gold) collide with such high energy that in a tiny volume, for about 10⁻²³ seconds, they create a state in which quarks are no longer confined in protons and neutrons. That is a restoration of the chiral symmetry that broke at t = 10⁻⁶ seconds after the Big Bang. We have literally recreated a state of the early universe — albeit in a region the size of an atomic nucleus and for an unimaginably brief time. It is not a full restoration of a phase transition in the sense of "all particles doing the same thing", but it is the closest we have ever come.

**What we (probably) will never be able to.** A 100 TeV accelerator (the proposed Future Circular Collider) could possibly restore the electroweak symmetry locally — a project of decades and hundreds of billions of euros, but physically feasible. A GUT transition requires an accelerator that would be the size of a galaxy. The Planck scale is practically unreachable; for that we must wait for nature itself, in black holes or the early universe, and hope that we can read the signals.

**The deeper observation.** The early universe was in a sense a sequence of phase transitions, comparable to how water-vapour-liquid-ice is a sequence, but on the scale of the universe as a whole and on timescales of fractions of fractions of seconds. What we observe today as "the laws of nature" are the frozen remains. At each cooling, one less symmetry, one more choice was made — until we ended up in the rich, structured universe we know, with distinctions between forces, between massive and massless particles, between matter and radiation. None of these structures existed at the beginning. They all arose by losing symmetries.

## Why this matters
The cascade of phase transitions tells us something deep about how the universe exists. The wealth of structure we see today — four different forces, a zoo of particles with different masses, hadrons that behave differently from quarks — is not an arbitrary collection of properties. It is what remained after a series of spontaneous choices the universe made as it cooled. Theoretically those choices are reversible, and the laws of nature themselves are more symmetric than our universe appears. We live in the ordered, asymmetric low-temperature phase of something that was once much more uniform. This holds at every scale: from the magnet on your fridge that only broke its symmetry at 770 °C, to the electroweak distinction between photons and W-bosons that did not yet exist at 10¹⁵ K. Everything in our world is, in a sense, a cooling product.

## Open questions
- Are there regions in a larger multiverse where the symmetry breakings turned out differently — with other forces, other particle masses, other natural constants?
- Is our current vacuum the true ground state, or "metastable"? Calculations based on the measured Higgs and top-quark masses possibly suggest the latter, on timescales of 10¹⁰⁰ years.
- How many phase transitions does the universe still have ahead of it? Could there be a future transition we cannot foresee?
