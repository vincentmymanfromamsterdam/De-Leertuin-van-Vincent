# When Earth did this before: volcanic CO₂ pulses and the PETM

## Core
Earth has undergone several rapid climate crises through volcanic CO₂ pulses, with the largest mass extinctions in history as a consequence. The most instructive example for the present situation is the Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum 56 million years ago: an emission comparable to our entire fossil fuel supply, a warming of 5-8 °C, and a hundred thousand years of recovery. What geologically distinguishes the current situation is not the end result — that is foreshadowed in history — but the speed at which we are heading there.

## Explanation
When you compare current climate change to geological history, you find not one but several periods in which CO₂ and methane rose rapidly through volcanic activity. Three are worth dwelling on.

**The Permian-Triassic extinction (252 million years ago) — the greatest extinction ever.** The cause was the Siberian Traps: a gigantic volcanic eruption that lasted hundreds of thousands to a million years, pouring millions of cubic kilometres of basalt across Siberia. CO₂ and sulphur dioxide were released in enormous quantities, plus methane when hot lava broke through hydrocarbon-rich sediments. The CO₂ level rose from about 400 ppm to perhaps 2,500 ppm. The consequences were catastrophic: 8-10 °C of global warming, ocean acidification, anoxic oxygen-starved oceans, massive forest die-off. About 95% of all marine species and 70% of all land animals disappeared.

**The Triassic-Jurassic extinction (201 million years ago).** The Central Atlantic Magmatic Province — the volcanic eruption that initiated the breakup of Pangaea — caused similar effects at smaller scale. CO₂ rises led to warming, ocean acidification, and the extinction of 30-40% of marine species.

**The most relevant example for now: the PETM.** The Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum, 56 million years ago, is geologically nearby and qualitatively comparable to what we are causing ourselves. In a period of 3,000 to 20,000 years — geologically fast — between 3,000 and 7,000 gigatonnes of carbon entered the atmosphere. That is comparable to what humanity would emit if we burned all remaining fossil fuels. The cause is disputed but probably a combination of volcanism in the North Atlantic region and the release of methane hydrates — frozen methane on the ocean floor — when those warmed. The consequences: global warming of 5-8 °C, deep-sea temperatures up by 4-5 °C, ocean acidification, extinction of benthic foraminifera, and the migration of subtropical ecosystems into the Arctic Circle. Climate recovery took 100,000 to 200,000 years.

**The crucial difference lies in speed.** PETM emitted 3,000-7,000 gigatonnes of CO₂ over 3,000-20,000 years — averaging 0.2 to 2 gigatonnes per year. Humanity now emits about 37 gigatonnes of CO₂ per year. We are therefore compressing an emission comparable in total to PETM, but at a pace 20 to 200 times faster. Even the Siberian Traps, which caused the greatest extinction ever, probably had a lower annual emission than PETM. We are, as far as the geological record knows, the fastest carbon emitter ever on this planet.

That changes how to interpret the current situation. Climate change is often presented as "unprecedented and unique", but that is not entirely scientifically accurate. The Permo-Triassic world had **higher** CO₂ levels than we are likely ever to reach. Earth has seen warmer periods in the past in which Antarctica was forest. What is geologically truly new is the **gradient** — the slope of the transition.

For ecosystems that makes all the difference. A species that gets 10,000 years to migrate northward usually makes it. A species that has to do it in 200 years often does not. Trees migrate about 100 metres per year under natural conditions. Climate zones now shift hundreds of kilometres in a human lifetime. The arithmetic simply does not work out, and that is where the greatest concern sits — not in the absolute temperature rise, but in the adaptation margin we give species.

**What we can learn from PETM.** Some groups of mammals underwent rapid dwarfism during PETM — horses, primates and other early placental mammals became 30% smaller. That suggests rapid adaptation is possible, but via body size and not via more complex traits. The geographical distributions of plants and animals changed dramatically. Tropical species appeared in what is now Wyoming and Belgium. Many ocean species disappeared — especially organisms that build calcium carbonate shells, because more acidic ocean water dissolves them. But the ecosystem as a whole functioned through, and after a hundred thousand years of gradual cooling a richly diversified biome emerged again.

PETM is therefore no comfort — many species went extinct, some biomes disappeared entirely, and recovery took longer than the entire human civilisation has existed. But it is also no apocalypse. It is the best historical analogue we have for what we are doing ourselves, only in slow motion.

## Why this matters
Knowing PETM and the older volcanic climate crises places the current situation in a sobering perspective. What we are doing is not without precedent — Earth has previously seen comparable total CO₂ emissions, comparable warmings, and comparable extinction waves. Earth survived each of those crises and reshaped its ecosystems. But three things are different now. First: our pace is 20-200 times faster than even PETM, which makes the adaptation margin for species extremely small. Second: we are not adding volcanism to an otherwise calm Earth, but on top of other stressors (habitat loss, pollution, invasive species). Third: as far as we know, we are the first species to consciously cause such a disturbance — and thus the first that can in principle choose to stop. That is no comfort but it is a unique position in geological history: not a powerless victim, but a responsible participant.

## Open questions
- Which specific physiological mechanisms allowed some species to survive PETM? Does that give hints which species now have more or less chance?
- Did the Siberian Traps and PETM volcanism have different chemical signatures we can distinguish from fossil-fuel CO₂, and what does that say about future climate recovery?
- Suppose we reduced our current emission rate to zero tomorrow — would we accelerate recovery by an order of magnitude, or is the inertia of the climate system already "baked in"?
