# Dark energy

## Core
The universe is not just expanding — that expansion is accelerating. The mysterious force responsible is what we call dark energy. It accounts for about 68% of the total energy content of the universe, and we have no idea what it is.

## Explanation
In 1998 two independent teams of astronomers discovered something baffling. They were studying distant supernovae (exploding stars of a type whose brightness is precisely known) to measure how fast the universe is expanding. The expectation was that the expansion would be slowing down — gravity, after all, pulls everything together. But the supernovae were dimmer than predicted, meaning they were further away than expected: the universe is expanding not more slowly but ever faster.

That is like throwing a ball upward and watching it accelerate further up rather than falling back. Something must be pushing the ball — something stronger than the gravity pulling it back.

We call that "something" dark energy, but the name is essentially a label for our ignorance. The simplest explanation is the cosmological constant — a property of empty space itself that exerts a constant outward pressure. Einstein originally introduced this constant in 1917 to make a static universe possible, later called it his "biggest blunder" when Hubble discovered the universe is expanding, but it turned out he was ironically right after all — just for a different reason.

Carroll explains how strange the situation is: when you try to calculate the cosmological constant from quantum mechanics, you get a value that is 10^120 times too large. That is the largest discrepancy between prediction and measurement in the entire history of science. There is clearly something fundamental we don't understand.

Together with [[Dark matter]], dark energy delivers a sobering message: of all the energy and matter in the universe, only 5% is the ordinary matter we know — atoms, stars, planets, you. The rest is dark.

## Why this matters
Dark energy determines the fate of the universe. If acceleration continues, galaxies will recede from each other ever faster until they vanish over the cosmic horizon. The observable universe will become emptier, colder, and lonelier. Whether another scenario is possible depends on the nature of dark energy — and we don't know that.

## Open questions
- Is dark energy truly constant, or does it change over time? (DESI measurements from 2024 may suggest the latter)
- Is the dark energy problem connected to the problem of quantum gravity? Both point to something fundamental we are missing.
