# The redshifted photon — where energy disappears

## Kern
A photon travelling through the expanding universe loses energy — its wavelength stretches with space. That energy goes nowhere. It is simply gone. This is not a bookkeeping error but proof that energy conservation is not an absolute law, exactly as Noether's theorem predicts.

## Uitleg
Imagine the purest experiment conceivable. One photon, alone in a vacuum, no interactions, no collisions. The photon travels through the universe. Space expands. The photon's wavelength stretches with it — growing longer, shifting from blue to red, from visible light to infrared, eventually to microwaves. Longer wavelength means lower energy. The photon loses energy, measurably and irreversibly.

The question your intuition immediately asks: where did that energy go? There is no particle absorbing it. No field getting warmer. No reservoir. The energy vanishes, and there is no violation of natural law — because the law that guarantees energy conservation does not apply here.

Noether's theorem proves that energy conservation follows from time symmetry: the assumption that the laws of physics work the same tomorrow as today. In a laboratory, on a planet, even in a galaxy, that symmetry is perfectly intact. But on cosmological scales, where space itself expands, the background on which the laws play is no longer static. Spacetime changes. Time symmetry is broken. And therefore energy conservation does not strictly hold.

Carroll puts it sharply: it is not that the photon "loses energy to space." Space is not a thing that can receive energy. It is that the concept "the total energy of the universe" is not a well-defined quantity in Einstein's general relativity. The bookkeeping that works flawlessly in a laboratory is not applicable on cosmic scales — not because we don't measure well enough, but because the mathematical structure of the theory does not permit it.

This feels deeply unsatisfying. In every experiment you can do on Earth, energy is conserved. No exceptions. That principle is so fundamental it feels like absolute truth. But it is not absolute truth — it is a consequence of a symmetry, and symmetries can break.

The flip side makes it even stranger. Dark energy — the mysterious force behind the accelerated expansion — has a constant energy density per volume. But the volume of the universe grows. That means the total dark energy *increases*. Where does it come from? Nowhere. For the same reason: the symmetry that guarantees energy conservation is broken.

Photons lose energy through expansion. Dark energy gains energy through expansion. They are two sides of the same coin — both consequences of an expanding spacetime in which Noether's time symmetry does not hold.

The cosmic microwave background is the most dramatic example. The photons emitted 380,000 years after the Big Bang had a temperature of roughly 3,000 K — glowing orange, visible light. Now, 13.8 billion years later, they have stretched to microwaves with a temperature of 2.7 K. The same photons, but with a thousand times less energy. That energy went nowhere. It disappeared into the growing space.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
This is the concept that closes and tests the entire chain built up in the vault. Noether's theorem says: symmetry → conservation law. Symmetry breaking shows that broken symmetries create structure. The synthesis note describes the universe as a cascade of symmetry breakings. And this photon example shows the ultimate consequence: even energy conservation — the most trusted law in all of physics — is not absolute but dependent on a symmetry that is broken on cosmic scales.

That is not a disappointment but a liberation. It shows that physics does not consist of unchangeable dogmas but of principles that depend on context. At your scale, energy conservation works flawlessly. At the scale of the universe, it does not. Both are true, and Noether's theorem explains why.

## Open vragen
- If energy conservation does not hold on cosmic scales, are there other conservation laws that are also broken by the expansion?
- The cosmic microwave background has been losing energy for 13.8 billion years. Is there a moment when the photons have so little energy that they effectively "vanish"?
- If dark energy increases while photons lose energy, is the universe heading toward a state consisting only of dark energy?
