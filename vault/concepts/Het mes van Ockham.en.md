# Occam's razor

## Core
*Entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem* — entities are not to be multiplied beyond necessity. Given two explanations that cover the same phenomenon equally well, pick the simpler one: fewer assumptions, fewer unseen entities, lighter theoretical weight.

## Explanation
The razor is named after William of Ockham (c. 1287–1347), an English Franciscan friar and logician. He used the principle sharply and consistently — hence the eponym — but he did not invent it. Aristotle and earlier scholastics formulated similar rules. Ockham's contribution was to hone it into a blade.

It is important to understand: the razor is a *methodological* prescription, not a *metaphysical* claim. It does not say "reality is simple" — it says "start with the simple hypothesis and add complexity only when the facts demand it". Russell admires this in Ockham: a sober, logical brake on the baroque entity-production of medieval scholasticism (angels, forms, essences, qualities — an entire taxonomic forest full of invisible beings, all "explaining" things on no evidence other than their conceptual convenience).

In modern science the razor turns up everywhere. Why we don't assume the Earth is flat: not because flat is impossible, but because a flat Earth requires a whole cascade of further assumptions (light-bending curves, conspiracies, physics that works differently at the horizon) to explain what we observe. A round Earth explains the same with less. The same goes for evolution versus special creation, heliocentrism versus epicycles, relativity versus the aether.

But the razor is not a truth detector. [[Dark matter]] and dark energy are not, on the face of it, parsimonious postulates — invisible entities that account for 95% of the universe — yet within the framework of general relativity they remain the simplest explanation for what we actually see (rotation curves, supernova data, the cosmic microwave background). "Simple" is always relative to a framework. What counts as needless multiplication in one paradigm may be the necessary minimum in another.

Combined with [[Hume's problem of induction|Hume's problem]], the razor takes on an interesting role. If we can't *prove* which hypothesis is right, we can at least *be sparing*. Parsimony is not a claim to truth, but a pragmatic rule of thumb where provable certainty fails.

## Why this matters
The razor teaches us that an explanation that "fits everything" often *fits too much* — the same way a conspiracy theory absorbs every counter-argument as part of the conspiracy. Real explanatory power lies not in flexibility but in restriction. A good theory rules things out. The razor is therefore not only a scientific tool but a daily thinking instrument: with every explanation you hear, ask whether a simpler one would cover the same ground.

## Open questions
- How do you measure "simplicity" objectively? Number of assumptions, or something else?
- Is the razor ultimately an aesthetic principle, a pragmatic principle, or something with ontological weight after all?
