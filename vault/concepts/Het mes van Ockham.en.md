# Occam's razor

## Core idea
*Entia non sunt multiplicanda praeter necessitatem* — entities are not to be multiplied beyond necessity. Given two explanations that cover the same phenomenon equally well, pick the simpler one: fewer assumptions, fewer unseen entities, lighter theoretical weight.

## Who and when
Named after William of Ockham (c. 1287–1347), an English Franciscan friar and logician. He used the principle sharply and consistently — hence the eponym — but he did not invent it. Aristotle and earlier scholastics formulated similar rules. Ockham's contribution was to hone it into a blade.

## What it does and doesn't say
The razor is a *methodological* prescription, not a *metaphysical* claim. It does not say "reality is simple" — it says "start with the simple hypothesis and add complexity only when the facts demand it". Russell admires this in Ockham: a sober, logical brake on the baroque entity-production of medieval scholasticism (angels, forms, essences, qualities).

In modern science it is everywhere. Why we don't assume the Earth is flat: not because flat is impossible, but because a flat Earth requires a whole cascade of further assumptions (light-bending curves, conspiracies, physics that works differently at the horizon) to explain what we observe. A round Earth explains the same with less.

## An honest caveat
The razor is not a truth detector. Dark matter and dark energy are not, on the face of it, parsimonious postulates — invisible entities that account for 95% of the universe — yet within the framework of general relativity they remain the simplest explanation for what we actually see (rotation curves, supernova data, the CMB). "Simple" is always relative to a framework.
