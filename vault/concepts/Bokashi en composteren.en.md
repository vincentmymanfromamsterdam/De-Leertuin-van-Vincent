# Bokashi and composting — two processes, one cycle

## Kern
Bokashi and composting are fundamentally different biological processes — fermentation versus aerobic decomposition — but they work best as two phases in the same cycle: bokashi as pre-digestion, composting as completion.

## Uitleg
Composting is aerobic: it works with oxygen and a broad community of bacteria, fungi, springtails, mites and earthworms. Temperatures rise to 65°C. The end product is humus — stable, pH-neutral to slightly alkaline, directly usable for the soil food web.

Bokashi is anaerobic: it works without oxygen, in a sealed bucket. The "effective micro-organisms" (EM) on the bokashi bran are mainly lactic acid bacteria, yeasts and phototrophic bacteria — a much narrower community. They ferment the material rather than decompose it, comparable to making sauerkraut. The end product is not compost but fermented organic material: acidic (pH 3-4), still recognisable in structure, and not directly usable for plants.

The magic lies in the combination. When fermented bokashi material goes onto an active compost heap, three things happen simultaneously.

First: decomposition accelerates. The fermented material is already "pre-digested" — cell structures have been broken down by lactic acid bacteria, making it easier for aerobic organisms to access. Bokashi material composts noticeably faster than fresh kitchen waste. The lactic acid bacteria die as soon as they are exposed to oxygen (they are obligate anaerobes), but the work they did remains.

Second: the pH shock is temporary. The bokashi material is acidic (pH 3-4), but an active compost heap has enormous buffering capacity. Aerobic bacteria and fungi neutralise the acid within days to weeks. As long as bokashi does not exceed 20-30% of the heap volume, acidification is not a problem. The heap itself produces alkaline by-products — ammonia from nitrogen breakdown, calcium carbonate from eggshells — that compensate for the acids.

Third: the soil food web benefits. The lactic acids are a food source for certain soil organisms. The moist, semi-digested material quickly attracts earthworms. And the combination yields a richer end product than composting alone, because fermentation makes additional nutrients available — especially phosphorus and potassium.

There is one point of attention. Adding a large amount of bokashi at once to a small, cold, low-activity heap can temporarily create an environment too acidic for earthworms — worms are sensitive to pH below 5. The solution is simple: mix with structural material (leaves, wood chips) and optionally add a handful of garden lime or eggshells as a buffer.

The real strength of bokashi is that it accepts kitchen waste that causes problems on a regular heap: cooked food, meat, fish, dairy. The fermentation suppresses rotting odours and kills most pathogens. On a regular compost heap, meat attracts rats and stinks; via bokashi it is safely and odourlessly pre-digested before it goes on the heap.

The cycle then becomes: kitchen waste → bokashi bucket (2-3 weeks anaerobic fermentation) → compost heap (months of aerobic decomposition) → humus → soil → mycorrhizal networks → plant roots → food → kitchen waste. A closed loop, driven by two complementary biological processes.

## Waarom het ertoe doet
It is one of the few places where you as a gardener consciously combine the two fundamental strategies of life — fermentation and respiration. Fermentation is the oldest metabolism on Earth (3.5 billion years old, from before oxygen entered the atmosphere). Aerobic decomposition is younger and more efficient. Bokashi → compost is, in effect, the evolution of life repeated in your back garden.
