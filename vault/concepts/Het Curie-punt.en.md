# The Curie point

## Core
Below a certain temperature, the electron spins in ferromagnetic materials such as iron spontaneously align and magnetism emerges. Above it, they point in random directions and the material is no longer magnetic. That critical temperature is the Curie point — and it is one of the finest everyday examples of spontaneous symmetry breaking.

## Explanation
Pierre Curie discovered the transition in 1895, well before quantum theory could explain it. He noticed that iron abruptly loses its magnetic properties at 770 °C — not gradually, but like a switch flipping. Above that threshold, a magnet is no longer a magnet.

The modern explanation lies in the electron spins. Every electron is a tiny magnet, and in ferromagnetic materials neighbouring spins can energetically reward each other for pointing the same way through the so-called *exchange interaction*. Below the Curie point that ordering force outweighs the thermal motion that would otherwise shake the spins into random orientations. The system spontaneously picks one direction — which direction does not matter, but once chosen it is locked in, and the entire block of iron has a net magnetisation. Above the Curie point thermal chaos wins and the spins point every which way at once: average magnetisation zero.

Not every metal has a Curie point. It is reserved for the four ferromagnetic elements — iron (770 °C), cobalt (1115 °C), nickel (358 °C), gadolinium (19 °C) — plus a handful of alloys and oxides (magnetite, Alnico, neodymium magnets). Copper, aluminium, gold, and silver are not ferromagnetic and therefore have no Curie point. Antiferromagnetic materials such as chromium have an analogous phase transition at their *Néel temperature*: there, neighbouring spins align in opposition, in an ordered chessboard pattern.

Curie temperatures vary wildly because the exchange interaction depends on the specific crystal lattice and electron configuration. Gadolinium loses its magnetism at room temperature — a gadolinium magnet in your hand simply switches "off". Cobalt, by contrast, remains magnetic up to nearly 1100 °C, which makes it indispensable for permanent magnets in high-temperature applications.

What makes the physics here so elegant is that the Curie point is a *phase transition* in the same mathematical sense as melting or boiling. Above Tc the system is rotationally symmetric — every direction is equivalent. Below Tc the system picks one and breaks that symmetry spontaneously. It is the same pattern that returns in [[Superconductivity]] (where electrons below a critical temperature choose to pair up), in liquid helium entering its superfluid state, and in a much deeper sense in the [[Higgs mechanism]] that gives particles their mass. The Curie point is therefore not a curiosity of solid-state physics but the most tangible instance of a principle that reaches into the foundations of particle physics.

## Why this matters
Most people know magnetism as a static property — an iron magnet is magnetic, full stop. But magnetism is in reality a *phase* the way liquid water and ice are phases, and that phase can be switched on and off with temperature. Heat a magnet above 770 °C and it is just a glowing lump of metal; cool it down and the magnetism returns on its own — but the direction of that new magnetisation is random, unless you hold an external magnetic field nearby. That is precisely how permanent magnets are "made" in the factory: heated in a strong external field, so they pick the right direction as they cool through their Curie point. The physics of a fridge magnet is literally symmetry breaking in action.

## Open questions
- Why do iron, cobalt, nickel, and gadolinium specifically have a ferromagnetic ground state while their neighbours in the periodic table don't?
- Is there a fundamental connection between the symmetry breaking at the Curie point and the symmetry breaking that gives particles their mass via Higgs — or is the mathematical similarity superficial?
