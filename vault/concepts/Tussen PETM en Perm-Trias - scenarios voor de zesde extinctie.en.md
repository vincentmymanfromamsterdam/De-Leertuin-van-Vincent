# Between PETM and Permian-Triassic: scenarios for the sixth extinction

## Core
The current extinction wave will almost certainly be worse than PETM, but what its upper bound becomes depends on choices made in the coming decades. In the optimistic scenario, extinction remains comparable to PETM levels around 5-15% of species. In the pessimistic scenario, where tipping points are activated and keep running, it can shift toward Permian-Triassic — the greatest extinction ever. Scientists no longer argue about whether a sixth extinction is happening; they argue about where in that range we end up.

## Explanation
Comparing on three variables that matter gives a sober picture of where the current situation stands relative to earlier climate crises.

| Variable | PETM | Permian-Triassic | Current (2024) | Projected 2100 (high-emission) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total CO₂ emission (Gt C) | 3,000-7,000 | 30,000+ | ~700 since 1850 | 2,000-5,000 |
| Emission rate (Gt C/yr) | 0.2-2 | <1 (spread out) | ~10 | possibly 15+ |
| Temperature rise | 5-8 °C | 8-10 °C | 1.2 °C | 2.5-4.5 °C |
| Timescale | 20,000 years | 1 million years | 200 years | 300 years |

In total CO₂ we sit between the two historic events. In rate we exceed both. That is the pivot point.

**Three scenarios are seriously projected by climate scientists and paleontologists.**

In the *rapid decarbonisation path* — warming to +1.5-2 °C, roughly what the Paris Agreement aims for — extinction outcome is probably comparable to or slightly above PETM. Five to fifteen percent of current species disappear over the coming centuries. Many coral reefs disappear, some polar ecosystems too, but no complete biome collapse. Recovery: about 100,000 years for diversity restoration.

In the *moderate path* — +2.5-3.5 °C, roughly where current policy projections land — it gets more serious. Extinction shifts to 25-30% of species. Permafrost begins to release methane and CO₂, a feedback that cannot be stopped once it starts. Ocean acidification attacks multiple classes of organisms. Recovery: perhaps 1-5 million years.

In the *high-emission path* — +4-5 °C or more — scientists seriously make the Permian-Triassic comparison. At this warming multiple feedbacks activate: permafrost methane, methane hydrates from the ocean floor, forest die-off that itself releases CO₂, ocean anoxia. Extinction outcome: possibly 50-75% of species over thousands of years. Whole biomes disappear — coral reefs, rainforests, alpine flora, glacier-edge habitats. Recovery: 10-30 million years, comparable to the great Phanerozoic extinctions.

**Three factors make even Permian-Triassic-like outcomes conceivable.**

First: speed stacks on other stressors. Earlier volcanic crises happened on an Earth without habitat fragmentation, pollution, invasive species, overfishing, or intensive agriculture. We are adding climate stress to an ecosystem already weakened. A species that without climate change might have survived can collapse under combined pressure. The sixth extinction is not a climate crisis as single cause — it is cascade amplification of existing disturbances.

Second: tipping points that feed themselves. The great extinctions were not caused by the original volcanic CO₂ alone, but by what came after: anoxic oceans, methane from warmer oceans, forest die-off releasing more CO₂. Those feedback loops could run for hundreds of thousands of years. We can trigger comparable loops — Arctic permafrost methane, Amazon drought, collapsing Antarctic ice shelves — that are then no longer dependent on our emissions. Once switched on, they run even if we stop tomorrow.

Third: ocean acidification is its own extinction route. One of the most lethal aspects of Permian-Triassic was not only warming but acidification. CO₂ dissolves in seawater and forms carbonic acid, which literally dissolves shells and coral reefs. Ocean pH has already fallen by 0.1 units since 1850 — a 30% increase in acidity. With unchecked emissions this can rise to 0.3-0.4 units, comparable to PETM levels. For shell-forming organisms — corals, shellfish, plankton that forms the base of the marine food chain — this is an existential threat.

**Three factors work in our favour relative to Permian-Triassic.**

The total CO₂ budget is bounded. Even if we burn all remaining fossil fuels, we will not exceed Permian-Triassic total emissions. The Siberian Traps spewed more carbon over a million years than all our reserves contain. End temperature will probably be lower than Permian-Triassic — only the transition is faster.

We can stop. Volcanoes do not stop on command. We can in principle bring our emissions to zero if there is the political will. That is a unique position in geological history. Whether we do so is another question, but the option exists.

Adaptive technology. We can actively relocate species, build climate corridors, artificially restore coral reefs, store seeds in gene banks. This is no complete safety net, but it shifts the odds for some species in a way that did not exist in earlier extinctions.

**The current consensus.** Given current policy trajectories we are on a path toward about 3 °C by 2100, possibly higher if feedbacks accelerate. Extinction outcome between PETM and Permian-Triassic, closer to PETM than Permian-Triassic. Estimates range from 20% to 50% species loss over the coming centuries. Full diversity recovery: 1-10 million years. That is precisely the range your question invokes — we sit on a trajectory between the two extremes, and which way we end depends on choices made in the next fifty years.

## Why this matters
The most serious climate science discussions are no longer about "whether or not climate change" but about "in which extinction range will we end up". That is a totally different question than ten years ago, and the answer depends directly on what humanity does in the coming decades. PETM happened over thousands of years through processes Earth could not stop. Our version happens over hundreds of years through processes we can in principle stop. Whether we do so determines our position between PETM and Permian-Triassic. That gives an unprecedented combination: an extinction event in which the cause, for the first time in the history of life, has a choice. Whether that choice is actually made depends not on physics but on politics, economics, and culture — three domains that fit in no climate model.

## Open questions
- Which emission path will we actually follow in coming decades, and how far do current policy projections diverge from what scenario 1 requires?
- Which specific tipping points have already been crossed or nearly so, and which can we still reverse?
- Is "species count" the right metric, or would functional biodiversity (which species fulfil which ecological roles) give a more serious picture?

## Further reading
- Anthony Barnosky, *Dodging Extinction* (2014) — Berkeley paleontologist who pioneered the scenario analysis
- IPCC AR6, Working Group II report (2022), chapter on ecosystems: [ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg2](https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg2/)
- Will Steffen et al., "[Trajectories of the Earth System in the Anthropocene](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1810141115)" — *PNAS* (2018), authoritative paper on tipping points and feedback loops
- David Wallace-Wells, *The Uninhabitable Earth* (2019) — fleshed-out high-emission scenario, controversial but scientifically grounded
- Peter Brannen, *The Ends of the World* (2017) — paleontological perspective on how the current situation compares to earlier extinctions
