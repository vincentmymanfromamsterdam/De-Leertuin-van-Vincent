# The birth of the four forces

## Core
The four fundamental forces — gravity, the strong nuclear force, the weak nuclear force, and electromagnetism — were once united in a single primordial force in the early universe. As the universe cooled, they split off one by one in a series of phase transitions. Mass itself was only born a third of the way through this cascade. But gravity has worked from moment one — not on mass, on energy.

## Explanation
In the first fraction of a second after the Big Bang, the universe was so hot and dense that none of the forces we know existed separately. Everything was one. As the universe expanded and cooled, this unity broke apart — not gradually but in sharp transitions, comparable to how water suddenly becomes ice at 0 °C or how iron loses its magnetism at its [[The Curie point|Curie point]]. Each force has its own transition temperature.

| Time | Temperature | Event |
|---|---|---|
| 10⁻⁴³ s (Planck time) | 10³² K | Gravity splits off from the other forces |
| 10⁻³⁶ s | 10²⁸ K | Strong nuclear force splits off (end of the GUT epoch) |
| 10⁻¹² s | 10¹⁵ K | Higgs freezes: weak and electromagnetic forces split, particles gain mass |
| 10⁻⁶ s | 10¹³ K | Quarks combine into protons and neutrons |

**Gravity went first.** At the Planck time — about 10⁻⁴³ seconds — gravity split off from the other forces. What exactly this means we don't know, because at those energy scales our theories break down. General relativity and quantum mechanics are not compatible in that regime, and we have no tested theory of quantum gravity. But according to the standard model, gravity has worked as a separate force from that moment on.

**Important detail: gravity couples to energy, not just to mass.** This is one of the finest insights from Einstein's general relativity. What curves spacetime is not "mass" but the **stress-energy tensor** — all forms of energy, momentum, pressure, and stress combined. Mass is just one very concentrated form of energy (E=mc² says: a lot of energy packed into very little volume). But light also carries energy, and therefore also gravity. A box full of photons weighs more than the same box empty, even though photons have no rest mass. This is no mathematical curiosity but an experimentally confirmed effect. In the very early universe, before a single particle had mass, there was enormous energy — and that created a strong gravitational field.

**Then the strong nuclear force.** Around 10⁻³⁶ seconds the strong force (which will later bind quarks into protons and neutrons) split off from what remained. This moment marks the end of the so-called GUT epoch (Grand Unified Theory). Inflation — the exponential expansion that cosmologists postulate to explain the smoothness of the universe — probably happened during this period.

**Then came Higgs.** Around 10⁻¹² seconds, at about 10¹⁵ K, the Higgs field "froze" into its low-energy state. Before this phase transition, quarks, electrons and the W and Z bosons were all massless. They flashed through the universe at the speed of light. After the freeze, they acquired their rest mass through interaction with the Higgs field. At the same time the electromagnetic and weak nuclear forces split from each other — before that they were one "electroweak" force. This is exactly the same mathematical pattern as at the Curie point: a symmetric high-temperature state that spontaneously breaks into an ordered low-temperature state.

**The rest is ordinary physics.** Quarks bind into hadrons (10⁻⁶ s), neutrons and protons fuse into the first atomic nuclei (3 minutes), electrons only capture those nuclei 380,000 years later (when light could finally travel freely — the cosmic microwave background we still measure today).

## Why this matters
The picture of "four forces that just existed" is incomplete. What we measure today as separate fundamental forces are the frozen remnants of a once simpler whole. The entire Standard Model of particle physics is essentially a catalogue of what emerged when those symmetries broke one by one. And within that lies a deeper insight: mass is not a primordial property of matter but a later invention — a consequence of interaction with the Higgs field, which itself only existed once the universe had cooled. It is as if the universe began with pure energy and motion, and only later invented "stuff". Gravity is older than matter itself.

## Open questions
- Will all four forces ever be reunited in a single theory, or are they fundamentally different? The search for a Theory of Everything turns on this question.
- What really happened during the Planck time? Without a tested theory of quantum gravity, that moment is a black box to us.
- Are there other fundamental forces we have not yet discovered? Dark matter and dark energy may hint at exactly this.
