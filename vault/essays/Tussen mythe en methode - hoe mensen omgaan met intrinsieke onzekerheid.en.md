# Between myth and method: how humans deal with intrinsic uncertainty

## Question/premise
Humans seem poorly equipped for deep uncertainty. We have been evolutionarily selected for rapid pattern recognition — spot the predator in the grass before it strikes. That speed costs something: we see patterns where none exist, we have a strong psychological need for certainty, and we often experience uncertainty as painful. And yet uncertainty is intrinsic to the human condition. We do not know what tomorrow brings. We do not know what our choices ultimately cause. We do not even know what underlies the world we perceive. The question running through this essay is how cultures, institutions and philosophies have related to this fundamental situation — and what that teaches us about our own time.

## Synthesis

Bertrand Russell opens his *History of Western Philosophy* (1945) with a distinction that has stayed with me for a long time. He describes the difference between "barbaric" and "civilised" ways of thinking, and hastens to add that he is not ranking populations. He means a psychological distinction: between reacting to immediate impulses and being able to hold on to abstract long-term goals. Sowing in March to harvest in September is for him the core gesture of civilisation — the willingness to do something now whose outcome is uncertain and far away.

What Russell is tracking here is that civilisation is in a sense a coping strategy for uncertainty. Not denial of it, not a solution to it — but a way to live in it nonetheless. From that angle, religion, science, scepticism and philosophy acquire a shared family resemblance. They are all attempts to approach the same fundamental puzzle, using different strategies, with different strengths and weaknesses.

**Religion: certainty via a higher source.** The oldest and broadest strategy. What we cannot know through our own faculties is revealed by a divine source. The future is in the hands of something that oversees it, so my uncertainty about it need not paralyse me. Death is not an end but a passage. Suffering has meaning within a larger plan. This is psychologically powerful because the strategy does not ask you to reason away the uncertainty — it only asks for trust in someone or something that oversees it for you. William James called this in *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902) not coincidentally the "religious experience": it is essentially a transformation of the relationship to uncertainty, not its elimination.

Russell, atheist that he was, treats religion in his book more fairly than one might expect. He sees that it performs social and psychological functions that secular alternatives struggle to replace. His critique is not that religion offers consolation, but that it claims to be knowledge while in reality being a coping strategy. That is an important distinction, and it explains why religion remains such a persistent phenomenon, even in secular societies.

**Science: staying uncertain in a structured way.** The modern scientific method is a second strategy that works fundamentally differently. Rather than seeking certainty in an external source, science accepts that individual observations are unreliable and builds a collective process — peer review, repeatability, falsification — that over time yields less unreliable conclusions. Science promises no certainty, but reduced uncertainty. An asymptote we never fully reach but do approach.

What Russell repeatedly emphasises: the scientific method was not invented as a certainty machine but as a way to remain structured uncertain. Newton did not know what gravity *was*; he could only describe how it behaved. That intellectual modesty is the core gesture of science, and it is precisely what distinguishes it from both dogmatic religion and dogmatic rationalism. As [[Hume's problem of induction|Hume]] saw, even this strategy rests on a foundation we cannot prove — that the future resembles the past. We *choose* to make that assumption because it is workable, not because we can justify it. [[Ockham's razor]] works on exactly the same footing: a pragmatic rule of thumb where provable certainty fails.

**Philosophy: handling uncertainty as conscious practice.** Russell sees philosophy itself as a strategy between religion and science. Not claiming access to revelation, but also not claiming that its conclusions are experimentally testable. Philosophy tries to think clearly about precisely the questions on which neither religion nor science gives a definitive answer.

In *The Problems of Philosophy* (1912) he writes that philosophy teaches us not what to think but how to think — especially how to think in the absence of certain knowledge. [[Stoicism — what you can and cannot control|Stoicism]] and [[Epicurus and the desire for tranquility|Epicureanism]] were in fact precisely that: methods for dealing with the fundamental uncertainty of life. How do I deal with death? With setbacks I cannot prevent? With the impossibility of knowing whether my choices are right? The Stoics chose the dichotomy of control: invest only in what is in your power, accept the rest. The Epicureans chose the elimination of unnecessary desires. Different routes, the same underlying question.

Russell's own position is ironic in this respect: he is a rationalist who accepts that rationality has its own limits. He chose the scientific method not because he believed it guarantees truth, but because it is the least bad strategy available. That is a philosophical position, not a scientific claim.

**Scepticism: uncertainty as principle.** A fourth strategy that sometimes seems confusingly close to science but is essentially different. Classical scepticism (Pyrrho, Sextus Empiricus) was not about "doubting everything" but about the suspension of judgement — *epoche* — about everything for which certain knowledge is impossible. Through this, paradoxically, one expected to reach the same tranquility the Stoics sought through acceptance.

Modern scepticism — including the [[Four forms of climate scepticism - what sceptics actually say|climate scepticism]] I analysed in an earlier note — often works differently. There, scepticism is not the suspension of judgement but the active challenging of consensus claims. That can have a healthy scientific function — science thrives on such disagreements, and the most serious sceptics (Curry, Lindzen) articulate uncertainty ranges that mainstream science sometimes presents too narrowly. But scepticism can also be a psychological strategy for dealing with uncertainty in a paradoxical way: by rejecting consensus, you reclaim control over your own beliefs. It is then a form of certainty-seeking disguised as doubt. The difference between these two modes of scepticism is hard to determine from outside, and possibly not always clear even to the sceptic themselves.

**Myth, art and story: meaning without answer.** A fifth strategy deserves separate mention because we easily overlook it. Stories, rituals, art and myth have played a role since the palaeolithic in how humans deal with uncertainty. Not by providing answers, but by creating meaning around questions that have no answer. This also works in secular modern cultures: literature about death does not make death less mysterious, but more bearable. A good poem about loss "solves" nothing, but it does change how you hold the loss. Joseph Campbell, Carl Jung and Mircea Eliade have written deeply on this — that myth is not primitive science, but a fundamentally different way of dealing with the unknown.

**The relationship between the strategies.** What stands out is that none of these five strategies can fully replace the others. Science gives no answer to "how should I live" or "what to do with my mortality". Religion offers no predictions about chemical reactions or orbital mechanics. Philosophy helps with both questions but solves neither. Scepticism keeps thinking sharp but gives no direction. Myth creates meaning but no knowledge. They are five instruments for five overlapping but distinct facets of the human relationship to uncertainty.

What Western culture has done in the last four centuries is an experiment in which combination of these strategies is workable. The scientific method has proved enormously successful for empirical questions. But what it has not produced is a replacement for the existential and social functions of religion. That has created a vacuum that various modern phenomena try to fill — political ideologies as religion-substitute, therapy as secular confession, consumerism as meaning-making, conspiracy thinking as pseudo-mythic explanation. With varying success.

Russell saw this coming. At the end of his *History* he writes that the loss of religious certainty has a price that is not always compensated by the gain of scientific knowledge. People need not only truth — they also need meaning, community, consolation. Science provides the first, not necessarily the other three.

## What this makes visible

If you lay all this out, you can characterise the modern situation as follows: we have by far the best instruments ever to reduce empirical uncertainty, but we live in an era in which the other strategies for handling uncertainty — religion, shared myths, tradition — are weakened. We are better informed than our ancestors, but at the same time more uprooted. Not because science is wrong, but because it addresses only one part of the problem.

That perhaps explains something of the paradoxical phenomenon that as scientific knowledge grows, irrational ideas do not disappear but shift. Climate scepticism, anti-vaccination, conspiracy theories, alternative medicine — none of these is well explained as "lack of information". They are better understood as attempts to reclaim control and meaning in a world in which the old strategies have eroded, and in which the new strategy provides knowledge but no answer to the psychological needs it does not address.

Russell's own answer to this puzzle was sober and partly unsatisfying, and he knew it. He argued that mature engagement with uncertainty consists of three elements. The first is intellectual honesty — not pretending to know what you do not know. The second is moral courage — acting on incomplete information because not acting is also a choice. The third is what he called "the love of wisdom" — a lifelong willingness to keep learning, to correct, to doubt, without becoming cynical.

That is not as psychologically powerful as religious faith. It does not provide warmth the way community myths do. But it is, according to Russell, what remains if you are honest about the human condition: we live with uncertainty, and the only workable attitude is not to deny that uncertainty but to accept it as given and to act as sensibly as possible within it. It is not an easy position, but it is an honest one.

What stands out about this position is that it does not come from one strategy, but from a balance between them. Russell's mature handling uses the scientific method for empirical questions, philosophical analysis for the choice-questions, a secular-stoic acceptance for what cannot be changed, and an open space for what myth and art can do that science cannot. None of the strategies is made absolute; each is applied to the questions for which it works. That is perhaps the hardest part — the willingness to figure out for each question which strategy fits, rather than applying one strategy as universal method.
